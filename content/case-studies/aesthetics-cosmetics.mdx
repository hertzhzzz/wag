## The Client
A boutique skincare importer based in Adelaide SA needed to launch a private label vitamin C serum line with a 3,000-unit order for a new product release. The company had been trying to source through 1688 and had already experienced significant delays.

## The Challenge
The client had worked with 3 previous 1688 suppliers, each of which failed GMP certification during the product development phase. These failures had delayed the product launch by 5 months and cost the business significantly in wasted sample fees and lost market timing. The client had limited ability to verify supplier credentials remotely and needed professional on-the-ground support in China to assess facilities before committing to production.

## How WAG Helped

### Step 1: Pre-screening and Factory Audit
WAG began by pre-screening 8 original equipment manufacturers (OEMs) in Guangzhou's Baiyun district. Using business license verification and factory photograph analysis, WAG's team eliminated 5 suppliers that showed significant red flags including mismatched registration documents, stock photo "facilities," and unverifiable certifications. The remaining 3 candidates were evaluated against GMP requirements specific to Australian cosmetic imports.

### Step 2: On-Site Factory Visit
WAG arranged a 2-day on-site audit of the most promising certified OEM in Guangzhou's Baiyun district. During the visit, WAG representatives documented production line conditions, checked certification documentation against original certificates, and collected physical samples of packaging materials being proposed for the serum line. The audit confirmed the facility held current GMP certification and had capacity for the 3,000-unit order within the client's timeline.

### Step 3: Documentation and TGA Pathway
With a verified manufacturer confirmed, WAG coordinated the TGA documentation pathway for the cosmetic product, working with an Australian sponsor company to ensure all required documentation met Therapeutic Goods Administration requirements for cosmetic imports. WAG reviewed all labeling claims against TGA advertising guidelines before production commenced.

## The Results

- GMP-certified manufacturer secured after 3 failed attempts through other channels
- Product launch rescheduled with confirmed production timeline
- TGA documentation pathway clarified and underway
- $47,000 saved compared to using an Australian distribution partner
- Zero quality issues reported across the 3,000-unit production run

## FAQ — Cosmetics Sourcing for Australian Businesses

**What is the GMP certification process for importing cosmetics to Australia?**

Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP) certification for cosmetics importing to Australia requires documentation from a TGA-recognized certification body. WAG helps clients verify Chinese manufacturers hold current GMP certification by checking directly with certification bodies, conducting on-site audits, and reviewing certification scope against the specific product type being sourced.

**Should I use OEM or ODM for my private label skincare business?**

OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturing) means the factory produces products based on your formulations and packaging specifications. ODM (Original Design Manufacturing) means the factory provides both formulations and packaging designs. For Australian skincare brands with specific formulations, OEM offers more control over ingredients and branding. WAG helps clients determine which model fits their business and sources appropriate factories in China.

**What are the minimum order quantities for private label cosmetics in China?**

Private label cosmetics minimum order quantities (MOQs) in China typically range from 1,000 to 5,000 units per SKU depending on the product type and packaging complexity. WAG negotiates flexible MOQs with Chinese factories by building relationships with manufacturers who accommodate smaller test runs, helping brands test products before committing to large production volumes.
