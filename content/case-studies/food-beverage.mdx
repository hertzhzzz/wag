## The Client
A specialty coffee roaster based in Adelaide SA had secured a significant contract to supply private label instant coffee to a distribution partner in Singapore. The order called for 2,400kg of private label instant coffee — a meaningful volume that justified direct sourcing from origin rather than purchasing through the Singapore distributor's mark-up pricing. The client needed to find a Chinese roaster capable of meeting both Chinese export documentation requirements and Singapore's food import regulations.

## The Challenge
The primary challenge the Adelaide roaster faced was navigating the difference between Chinese domestic "food grade" certifications and international food safety standards. Several Yunnan roasters they contacted claimed to have "food grade" certification, but when WAG's team reviewed documentation, it became clear that domestic Chinese food safety certifications did not automatically meet the standards required for export to Singapore. Additionally, Singapore's Agri-Food and Veterinary Authority (AVA) has specific documentation requirements including HACCP certification, Certificate of Analysis (COA), and health certificates that needed to be prepared correctly for submission.

### Industry Background
Food and beverage exports from China present specific verification challenges that differ from other product categories. The regulatory frameworks governing food safety vary significantly between countries, and certifications that satisfy domestic Chinese requirements may not meet the documentation standards expected by importing countries. For Australian businesses sourcing food products from China or exporting Australian food products to third-country markets, understanding these regulatory differences is essential.

The distinction between domestic "food grade" certification and international food safety standards creates particular confusion. Chinese food safety certification systems have their own requirements and testing protocols, but holding domestic certification does not automatically mean a facility meets the standards expected by Singapore's AVA, the Australian Department of Agriculture, or other international food safety authorities. The terminology overlaps in ways that can mislead buyers who do not understand what each certification actually covers.

HACCP certification has become the international standard for food safety management, recognised by regulatory authorities across major food import markets. A facility with genuine HACCP certification from an accredited body has established documented food safety processes that meet internationally accepted requirements. However, the accreditation status of the certification body matters. Certificates issued by non-accredited bodies do not carry the same regulatory acceptance as those from recognised certification organisations.

Export documentation requirements add further complexity. Health certificates, certificates of analysis, and origin documentation must be prepared in formats acceptable to destination country authorities. Errors or omissions in export documentation can result in customs holds, rejections, or re-export requirements that add significant cost and delay to international food shipments. Professional verification ensures documentation meets destination country requirements before products leave the production facility.

## FAQ — Food and Beverage Exports for Australian Businesses

**What HACCP certification is required for exporting food products to Singapore?**

Singapore's AVA (Agri-Food and Veterinary Authority) requires food exporters to provide HACCP (Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points) certification from a recognized certification body. Chinese exporters must hold current HACCP certification from an accredited certification body, and this certification must be documented in the export documentation package. WAG verifies HACCP certification validity directly with certification bodies before recommending suppliers.

**What are the Australian food import requirements for Chinese food products?**

Chinese food products imported to Australia must comply with the Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code and be accompanied by documentation demonstrating food safety compliance. Products must come from manufacturers approved by AQSIQ/CAIC (now CNCA) and registered with the Australian Department of Agriculture. WAG helps clients understand the specific documentation and compliance requirements for their product categories.

**What are typical minimum order quantities for private label coffee from Chinese roasters?**

Private label instant coffee MOQs from Chinese roasters typically start at 500-1,000kg for initial production runs, with many factories accommodating 1,000-2,000kg for international private label orders. Some facilities offer smaller runs for test batches at higher per-unit pricing.
