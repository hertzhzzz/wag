## The Client
An allied health clinic group operating 3 locations across Sydney NSW required medical grade PPE including N95 respirators and protective gowns for their clinical staff. The order comprised 50,000 units across the 3 locations, representing a significant procurement investment. With healthcare settings requiring TGA-registered medical devices, the clinic group needed to ensure any supplier they worked with could provide genuine documentation meeting Australian regulatory requirements.

## The Challenge
The clinic group had been approached by multiple suppliers claiming to have TGA-registered medical PPE available for immediate dispatch. However, when the clinic group's procurement team attempted to verify supplier claims against the TGA's Australian Register of Therapeutic Goods (ARTG), they found significant discrepancies that they could not resolve independently. With 3 of 4 suppliers presenting fraudulent TGA registration documents, the procurement team faced the real risk of importing non-compliant PPE that could not legally be used in clinical settings — or worse, would fail to protect clinical staff during patient care.

The Therapeutic Goods Administration maintains the Australian Register of Therapeutic Goods (ARTG), which is the official record of devices approved for use in Australia. Any medical device imported for clinical use must have a corresponding ARTG entry, meaning that supplier claims about TGA registration must be verifiable against this public record.

The market for medical PPE, particularly during periods of supply shortage, attracts opportunistic suppliers whose documentation may not reflect genuine regulatory status. The Sydney clinic group's experience with four of six suppliers presenting fraudulent TGA certificates illustrates a broader pattern where documentation fraud becomes more prevalent during periods of high demand. Australian customs requirements for medical device imports include verification that the imported products match their ARTG registration. For clinical organizations, receiving non-compliant products means procurement funds spent on unusable inventory, extended delays while sourcing compliant alternatives, and potential patient care impacts if essential PPE supplies are delayed.

## FAQ — Medical Device Imports for Australian Healthcare Businesses

**What is the TGA pathway for importing medical devices to Australia?**

The TGA (Therapeutic Goods Administration) pathway for importing medical devices requires an Australian Sponsor company to manage ARTG (Australian Register of Therapeutic Goods) registration, provide conformity assessment documentation, and ensure all devices meet relevant standards. Medical devices must be included on the ARTG before importation, and suppliers must hold valid TGA ARTG entries that match the specific product variants being imported.

**How do I verify TGA registration status for a Chinese medical device supplier?**

TGA ARTG registration can be verified by cross-referencing certificate numbers against the TGA's public search database available on their website. Key verification steps include confirming the ARTG entry number, checking that the registration is current and not expired, verifying the supplier name matches the ARTG entry holder, and confirming the specific product variant is covered under the registration. WAG conducts this verification for all medical device suppliers before recommending them to clients.

**What documentation is required for importing medical PPE to Australia?**

Medical PPE imports require TGA ARTG registration documentation, conformity assessment documentation, Australian Sponsor details, batch-specific quality documentation, and for some product types, clinical evidence of safety and performance. WAG assembles complete documentation packages that meet TGA requirements and coordinates with Australian Sponsors to ensure compliance before shipment.
