## The Client
A boutique fashion retailer operating out of Fitzroy, Melbourne needed to launch a private label women's wear line for the upcoming season. Their design concept called for a small test run of 90 units — 30 units per colourway across 3 colour options — to validate market demand before committing to larger production volumes. Local Australian manufacturers had consistently quoted 500-unit minimums per colourway, making a small test run financially unviable through domestic suppliers.

## The Challenge
The fashion retailer's primary challenge was Minimum Order Quantity (MOQ) expectations. Every Australian manufacturer they approached quoted 500-unit minimums per colourway, which would require a 1,500-unit commitment to test their 3-colourway concept. This represented significant financial risk for a new product line launch. The client needed to source from manufacturers comfortable with smaller runs while maintaining the quality standards their retail customers expected. Without access to manufacturing networks in China, they had no clear pathway to the flexible production arrangements they needed.

## How WAG Helped

### Step 1: Factory Mapping and Research
WAG's team mapped 12 garment factories across Guangzhou's Panyu district and Zhongshan's textile markets — regions known for flexible small-batch manufacturing. This research identified factories with track records of accommodating smaller runs for international clients, as well as those with established export experience to English-speaking markets.

### Step 2: MOQ Negotiation
Rather than accepting initial factory quotes at face value, WAG negotiated directly with factory owners on behalf of the client. Through a combination of demonstrating serious buyer intent, offering faster payment terms, and explaining the potential for larger follow-up orders if the test run succeeded, WAG secured flexible MOQ arrangements with 2 factories willing to produce 30 units per colourway — exactly matching the client's requirements.

### Step 3: Fabric Sourcing Delegation
With production arrangements confirmed, WAG arranged a fabric sourcing delegation to Zhongshan's fabric market. Accompanied by a professional interpreter with fashion industry experience, the client was able to evaluate fabric quality directly, select materials appropriate for their designs, and establish relationships with fabric suppliers who could provide the quality of materials their brand required.

## The Results

- 90-unit test run completed successfully with 30 units per colourway
- Landed cost of $28 per unit compared to $67 from Australian pattern makers
- Fabric quality verified through direct sourcing visit
- Test run sold through within 6 weeks of receipt
- Follow-up order of 300 units placed with the same factory

## FAQ — Garment Sourcing for Australian Fashion Businesses

**How do I find garment factories in China that accept small MOQs?**

Garment factories in China's Guangzhou Panyu and Zhongshan regions are known for flexible MOQ arrangements, with many factories accepting runs as low as 30-50 units per style for established clients. Building relationships directly with factory owners and demonstrating serious buyer intent improves MOQ flexibility. WAG maintains factory relationships across these regions and negotiates MOQ terms on behalf of Australian clients.

**What is the typical MOQ for private label clothing from Chinese manufacturers?**

Private label clothing MOQs from Chinese manufacturers typically range from 100-500 units per style for standard production runs. However, factories in established export-oriented manufacturing zones often accommodate smaller test runs of 30-100 units, particularly for clients with established relationships or who offer favorable payment terms. WAG helps Australian brands negotiate MOQ terms that match their business requirements.

**What Australian compliance requirements apply to imported apparel labeling?**

Imported apparel sold in Australia must comply with the Trade Marks Act and Australian Consumer Law regarding fiber content labeling, country of origin declarations, and care instruction requirements. Labels must be in English and provide accurate fiber content percentages. WAG reviews labeling documentation during production planning to ensure compliance before shipment.
