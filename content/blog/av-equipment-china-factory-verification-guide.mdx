# How to Verify Chinese Factories for AV Equipment: The 3-Step Guide Australian Brands Need

If you are an Australian AV integrator, pro audio retailer, stage lighting company, or event production business sourcing equipment from China, one question matters more than any other:

**Is this supplier actually a factory?**

Not a trading company that brokers orders. Not a middleman that farms out to whoever accepts the lowest price. An actual manufacturing facility with production lines, workers, and the capability to make what they are selling.

The distinction matters because your deposit, your timeline, and your reputation are all built on that answer.

The core challenge is structural. Australian businesses face three barriers that make independent verification nearly impossible:

1. **Language barriers** -- Chinese business documents, government databases, and certification systems are not accessible without Mandarin fluency
2. **Geographic distance** -- You cannot pop round to verify in person without significant travel investment
3. **Trust deficit** -- Without an existing relationship, how do you know what they show you is real?

Most Australian companies solve this by using Alibaba.com or similar platforms. Here is what the data shows:

- **85% of Australian SMBs** use Alibaba to find overseas suppliers
- **China accounts for 62%** of all inquiry sources for Australian importers

But using Alibaba introduces a different problem: the platform is dominated by trading companies, not factories. A trading company can have an impressive website, fluent English, professional sample photos, and still be nothing more than an order broker.

The Alibaba Verified badge means a third party verified the company exists. It does not verify what the company actually does, whether they manufacture, or if they will still be operating when you need them.

## The 3-Step Framework to Verify Any Chinese Factory

Based on our work with Australian AV companies across pro audio, stage lighting, events, and church AV installations, we have developed a three-step verification process that works before you send any deposit.

![3 Steps to Verify Any Chinese Factory](/social/linkedin-post/2026-04-01-av-verification/imgs/02-three-step-verification-framework.png)

### Step 1: Check the Business License on gsxt.gov.cn

The National Enterprise Credit Information Publicity System (gsxt.gov.cn) is China equivalent of the Australian Business Register. Every legitimate Chinese business must register here, and the filings are public.

**What to look for:**

- **Business scope** -- Does it include "manufacturing" (生产) or "production" (制造)? If the scope only shows "wholesale" (批发) or "trade" (贸易), you are looking at a trading company, not a factory.
- **Registered capital** -- Higher capital suggests greater investment, but this alone is not guarantees
- **Legal representative** -- This is the person legally responsible for the business

**How to use gsxt.gov.cn:**

1. Go to gsxt.gov.cn
2. Enter the company name in Chinese (you may need to ask the supplier for this)
3. Review the business license screenshot
4. Look specifically for manufacturing or production in the business scope

This step takes 10 minutes and costs nothing. If the supplier refuses to provide their Chinese business name for verification, that is your first red flag.

### Step 2: Request Live Video of Production During Working Hours

Professional photos can be staged. Sample rooms can be rented for a day. Even factory tour videos can be carefully curated.

What cannot be faked: **live footage of active production lines during normal working hours**.

When we verify factories for clients, we specifically request:

- A video call during China business hours (Monday to Friday, 9am-6pm China time)
- The video must show active production -- workers at stations, machinery running
- We ask to see raw materials entering one end of the production line and finished goods at the other

This step matters because trading companies do not have active production lines. They place orders with factories and supervise quality from the sidelines. When you ask for live production evidence, they have two options: admit they are a trading company, or try to fake it (which skilled investigators can detect).

### Step 3: Verify Certifications Directly with the Issuing Body

The third and most critical step: do not accept certificate files at face value.

Trading companies routinely provide fabricated or outdated certifications. The CE marking, ISO certifications, and product test reports that look impressive in a PDF file may have expired, been revoked, or never belonged to the products they claim to certify.

**How to verify independently:**

1. Note the certification body name from the certificate
2. Visit the certification body website directly (not a link from the supplier)
3. Use their verification lookup tool (most major certifiers have these online)
4. Enter the certificate number to confirm validity
5. Check: does the certified company name match the supplier?

This step catches the majority of verification failures. In our experience, approximately 30% of certificates presented by Chinese trading companies fail direct verification with the issuing organization.

## What Australian Consumer Law Means for Your Import

Under Australian Consumer Law, when you import goods from overseas, you are the **importer of record**. This carries specific legal responsibilities:

- You are legally responsible for compliance with Australian standards
- You cannot fully transfer liability to your supplier
- Product liability claims may fall on you if the supplier cannot be held accountable

This is why verification matters beyond operational concerns. If you import equipment that fails to meet Australian standards, and the supplier disappears or denies responsibility, you absorb the full cost.

Working with a guide who understands both Australian legal requirements and Chinese business practices significantly reduces this exposure.
