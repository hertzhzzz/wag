Machinery purchases require longer due diligence than consumer goods. A missed deadline on a clothing order costs you a customer. A missed deadline on a production line costs you an entire operation.

Australian businesses sourcing industrial equipment from China face specific challenges: verifying machine performance before purchase, confirming after-sales support exists, and ensuring the equipment meets Australian safety and electrical standards. This guide covers what our team has learned from accompanying Australian businesses on machinery factory visits across Guangdong, Jiangsu, and Shandong provinces.

## Key Machinery Manufacturing Regions in China

**Pearl River Delta (Guangzhou, Shenzhen, Dongguan)** produces general manufacturing equipment including CNC machine tools, industrial automation equipment, packaging machinery, printing and labelling equipment, and plastic injection moulding machines. The region's ecosystem density means component suppliers are nearby, reducing lead times and logistics complexity.

**Yangtze Delta (Shanghai, Jiangsu, Zhejiang)** focuses on precision and heavy industry: precision machining centres, industrial robots, textile machinery, agricultural machinery, and mining and construction equipment. Jiangsu province in particular has established export capabilities for mining equipment with quality systems that meet international standards.

**Beijing-Tianjin Region** serves heavy industry and aerospace with large-scale production lines and industrial heating equipment. For Australian buyers, this region is less frequently accessed but relevant for specialised heavy machinery.

## What to Verify in a Machinery Factory

### Manufacturing Capability

Production floor inspection reveals what photographs cannot. Walk the production floor during working hours—are machines actively running? Idle equipment may indicate the factory is operating below claimed capacity or is not the actual manufacturer.

Operator skill matters significantly for precision machinery. Ask about worker training programmes and tenure. A factory with high worker turnover produces inconsistent results.

Quality management system certification (ISO 9001) indicates a structured approach to quality, but verify the certificate independently through the issuing body. Do not accept file copies.

Machine inventory tells you whether the factory can actually manufacture what they are selling. A factory claiming to produce CNC machines should have CNC machines on the floor, not just assembly benches.

### Performance Data

Never purchase machinery on specifications alone. Request evidence: performance testing results from machines they produce, machines running in operation rather than static demonstration, reference customers with contact details (particularly in your industry), and production samples using your materials.

Bring a technical specification sheet with the tolerances that matter for your application. Ask the factory to run a test using your material and measure the output against your specification. A legitimate manufacturer will accommodate this request. A supplier who cannot demonstrate performance is a supplier whose claims are unverified.

### Component Quality

Machinery performance depends on component quality. Ask specifically: who supplies the key components (motors, drives, PLC controllers, sensors)? Do they use established international brands or in-house and domestic components? What is the failure rate of key components? Can you receive a component list with brand names before purchase?

In our experience, Australian buyers underestimating the importance of component sourcing have received machinery where the specified components were substituted with cheaper alternatives post-order, with no notification. Document your component requirements explicitly in purchase agreements.

## Australian Safety and Compliance Requirements

**Safe Work Australia** establishes workplace health and safety requirements. Specific AS/NZS standards apply to different machine types—know which standard applies to your category before you engage a supplier.

Electrical safety must meet Australian electrical standards. CE marking is not automatically accepted in Australia. A machine certified for the European market may require modifications for Australian electrical systems.

Ask specifically: "Can you provide documentation showing this machine meets Australian electrical and safety standards, or can you specify what modifications would be required for Australian compliance?"

Request: operating manual in English, electrical schematics, safety circuit diagrams, maintenance procedures, spare parts list with part numbers, and declaration of conformity.

For machinery purchases above AUD 50,000, an independent inspection by a third-party engineering company is strongly recommended. This is different from consumer goods inspection—you need someone with specific machinery expertise who can verify mechanical performance, electrical safety, and compliance documentation.

## After-Sales Support

Before purchase, clarify: warranty terms (duration, what is covered, who pays for shipping on warranty claims), spare parts availability (how quickly can parts be supplied internationally?), technical support (English-speaking support available, support hours), installation and commissioning (does the factory offer installation services or just shipping?), and remote support capability (can they diagnose faults via video or remote connection?).

Get the name and contact details of the factory's after-sales manager before you leave. If they do not have a dedicated after-sales function, that is a significant risk for a machinery purchase.

A WAG client in Western Australia purchased injection moulding equipment from a Foshan manufacturer in 2024. The machine arrived with a manufacturing defect on the hydraulic system. The factory had no after-sales staff and did not respond to warranty claims. Repair costs in Australia exceeded AUD 8,000. A pre-purchase question about after-sales support would have revealed this gap before the order was placed.

## Red Flags in Machinery Sourcing

| Red Flag | What It Signals |
|----------|-----------------|
| No test data available | Machine has not been validated |
| Refuses to show production floor | Not the actual manufacturer |
| No after-sales support function | Post-purchase risk |
| Cannot name key component suppliers | Component quality unknown |
| Price significantly below market | Quality compromise or false representation |
| No export experience to Australia | Unfamiliar with compliance requirements |

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What MOQs apply to machinery?**

Unlike consumer goods, machinery is typically sold as single units. However, most factories will offer better pricing for two or more units. For highly customised machinery, the first unit may require significant setup time that makes it relatively expensive; subsequent units of the same specification become cheaper.

**How long does machinery production take?**

Lead times vary widely: off-the-shelf equipment may ship within 2-4 weeks. Custom-built or complex machinery can take 3-6 months. Build this into your project timeline and do not commit to tight delivery schedules before confirming with the factory.

**Should I arrange an independent inspection?**

For machinery purchases above AUD 50,000, an independent inspection by a third-party engineering company is strongly recommended. This is different from consumer goods inspection—you need someone with specific machinery expertise who can verify mechanical performance, electrical safety, and compliance documentation.

**How do I verify Australian electrical compliance before purchase?**

Request documentation showing the machine meets Australian electrical standards. Ask specifically whether CE-certified equipment requires modification for Australian compliance. Engage a licensed electrician in Australia to review the factory's technical documentation before committing.

**What after-sales support should I require in the purchase agreement?**

Specify: warranty duration (minimum 12 months for machinery), what components are covered, response time for technical support requests, availability of spare parts internationally, and whether after-sales support is provided in English.
