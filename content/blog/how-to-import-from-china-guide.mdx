Importing from China is not technically complex, but it has enough moving parts that missing a step at the beginning — the wrong supplier, an incorrect tariff classification, an missing quarantine clearance — creates problems that are expensive and time-consuming to fix after goods are on the water. This guide covers what Australian businesses actually need to do, in the order they need to do it, based on our direct experience of placing and managing orders from Chinese factories.

## Understanding the Import Process

The first thing to understand is that the import process is sequential. Skipping steps, or doing them out of order, is where most problems originate. The correct sequence is: understand what you are importing and what it costs, verify your supplier, negotiate your terms, document everything, arrange inspection, then arrange shipping.

Most Australian businesses that have problems with their first China import had a problem with step two or three that they did not know about at the time. A supplier that seemed verified turned out not to be a manufacturer. A price that seemed competitive turned out to not include the things that made it comparable to other quotes. A lead time that seemed reasonable turned out to assume a perfect production run with no contingencies.

Understanding the process before you start is the highest-return activity in China sourcing. It takes a few hours of reading. Getting it wrong costs thousands.

## Step 1: Register Your Business for Import

Before you can import anything into Australia, your business needs to be set up to do so.

**Australian Business Number (ABN):** Every business importing goods into Australia must have an ABN. If your business does not already have one, register through the Australian Business Register. The ABN application is straightforward and typically processes within minutes for new businesses.

**Import Client Account with Australian Border Force:** This is separate from your ABN. The Import Client Account allows you to lodge import declarations and access the ABF's systems for tracking shipments. You apply through ABF's Import Client Account registration process. Allow two to three weeks for processing.

**Registering as an Importing Business:** Your business must be registered as an importing entity with the ABF. This involves completing the relevant forms and providing your ABN and business registration documents. Your accountant can typically handle this registration if you are not familiar with the process.

Without these registrations in place, goods will be held at the port when they arrive. There is no workaround and no expedite process that shortcuts this requirement.

## Step 2: Understand What You Are Importing

Every product imported into Australia is classified under the Australian Customs Tariff system. The tariff classification determines your duty rate and whether the product is subject to additional requirements.

**Tariff Classification:** Products are classified under the Harmonized System (HS) codes, an internationally standardised system used by more than 200 countries. Australia uses an eight-digit HS code structure. The first six digits are internationally consistent; the seventh and eighth digits are Australia-specific. Incorrect tariff classification is one of the most common errors in customs clearance — and it can result in overpayment or underpayment of duties, both of which create problems.

Use the Australian Border Force's tariff classification search tool or engage a customs broker to confirm your product's HS code. The cost of a customs broker's advice on classification is trivial compared to the cost of a customs dispute.

**Duty Rates under ChAFTA:** The Australia-China Free Trade Agreement (ChAFTA) provides preferential duty rates for many product categories imported from China. Under ChAFTA, some products enter Australia at zero duty; others receive significant reductions compared to the standard Most-Favoured-Nation (MFN) rate. To claim ChAFTA preferences, you need a Certificate of Origin from your Chinese supplier confirming that the goods meet the applicable rules of origin.

**GST:** Goods imported into Australia are subject to Goods and Services Tax at 10 percent on the customs value. The customs value includes the purchase price, freight costs to Australia, and insurance. If your business is registered for GST, you can claim input tax credits on the GST paid at customs.

**Quarantine Requirements:** Australia has some of the strictest biosecurity requirements in the world. Products made from plant materials, wood packaging, food products, animal products, or biological materials require specific treatment and documentation. Wooden packaging must comply with ISPM 15 standards — heat treatment or fumigation with official marking. Failure to comply with quarantine requirements results in goods being refused entry or treated at the importer's expense.

Use the Biosecurity Import Conditions database (BICON) to determine the specific requirements for your product. If your product contains any component of biological origin — wood, plant fibre, animal product — check BICON before placing your order.

## Step 3: Find and Verify Chinese Suppliers

Finding suppliers in China is not difficult. Alibaba, Made-in-China, Global Sources, and the Canton Fair all provide access to thousands of factories and trading companies. The difficulty is distinguishing genuine manufacturers from trading companies, and distinguishing reliable suppliers from those that will take your deposit and not deliver.

**Start with the right type of supplier.** The most important distinction is between a factory and a trading company. A factory manufactures your product. A trading company sources it from a factory and resells it to us. Trading companies are not inherently dishonest, but they add a layer of cost and reduce your access to the people who actually make your goods. For most Australian businesses, direct factory relationships produce better outcomes: clearer communication, better pricing, stronger quality control.

How do you tell a factory from a trading company? A genuine factory will have a production facility where your product is manufactured. They will be able to show you the production line. They will own the tooling and production equipment. They will have workers on the floor doing manufacturing tasks. A trading company will have a showroom, an office, and relationships with factories — but they are not the manufacturer.

**Verify the supplier's business registration.** Every legitimate Chinese business has a business licence registered with China's State Administration for Market Regulation (SAMR). You can verify a Chinese company's registration through the National Enterprise Credit Information Publicity System at gsxt.gov.cn. This is a free, public database. A factory that cannot be verified through this system, or whose registration details do not match what they have told you, is not a reliable trading partner.

**Request samples before committing.** No serious order should be placed without a physical sample approval. Samples allow you to verify that the factory can produce to your specifications, not just to a description. Request samples of your actual product — not a similar product they happened to have in their showroom.

**The most reliable verification is an in-person factory visit.** Our experience across more than 200 factory visits is that approximately one in three factories that appears legitimate on paper has a significant issue that is visible on the floor: mismatched registered addresses, inactive production lines, no export documentation, or certifications that do not match the product. No document review can replace standing on a production floor and watching it run.

## Step 4: Negotiate and Document Your Purchase

Chinese factory negotiations operate differently from Australian commercial negotiations. The price quoted is not a final price — it is an opening position in a conversation about whether and how you will work together. The most successful Australian importers approach negotiations as relationship-building conversations rather than adversarial price contests.

**Open with interest, not demands.** When you open a negotiation by asking "Can you do better on price?" the factory hears: "We are not sure we want to work with you." When you open by asking about the factory's production experience, their current order book, their lead times for your product category, the factory hears: "This buyer is serious and wants to understand how we work." The latter is a more productive conversation.

**Negotiate the full package.** Price is only one variable. Minimum order quantity, lead time, tooling costs, payment terms, warranty provisions, and packaging requirements are all negotiable. A factory that will not move on unit price may be flexible on MOQ. A factory with a long lead time may be able to compress it if payment terms are adjusted. The value you capture by negotiating the full package typically exceeds what you would achieve by focusing only on unit price.

**Get everything in writing.** Verbal agreements, WeChat messages, and email confirmations are not purchase contracts. A Chinese supplier relationship without a formal written purchase agreement is not a commercial relationship — it is a sequence of informal understandings that are difficult to enforce. Your written agreement should specify unit price by quantity break point, exact minimum order quantity, lead time from deposit to ready-for-shipment, tooling and setup costs and ownership terms, payment terms including deposit percentage and balance timing, quality specifications referenced to your approved sample, warranty and defect resolution provisions, and dispute resolution mechanism.

## Step 5: Manage Production and Quality

Once your order is placed and your deposit is paid, production begins. The period between deposit and shipment is when quality problems most commonly emerge — and when they are still fixable before the goods leave China.

**Confirm your sample before production begins.** Your approved sample is your quality reference standard. Everything produced must match it. Do not allow production to begin until the sample is formally approved in writing. We have seen factories begin production before sample approval because they were eager to meet a lead time — the result was goods that did not match specifications and a dispute over who approved what.

**Request production updates.** Most factories will provide periodic updates if asked. Photographs of production progress, video of the production line, and documentation of quality checks during production are reasonable requests from a serious buyer. A factory that is reluctant to provide updates has something to hide.

**Arrange pre-shipment inspection.** Before goods are shipped, arrange an independent inspection through SGS, Bureau Veritas, or a similar internationally recognised inspection company. The inspector visits the factory, checks goods against your approved sample and agreed specifications, and provides a report before you release the balance payment. This is the last point at which a quality problem can be fixed before it becomes your problem at the Australian port. For orders above AUD 20,000, we treat pre-shipment inspection as non-negotiable.

## Step 6: Arrange Shipping and Customs Clearance

**Sea freight vs. air freight:** Sea freight from major Chinese ports (Shenzhen, Guangzhou, Shanghai) to Australian ports (Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, Adelaide, Perth) typically takes three to four weeks. Full Container Load (FCL) provides the lowest per-unit cost for larger orders. Less than Container Load (LCL) is appropriate for smaller volumes and is charged by cubic metre. Air freight costs significantly more but takes three to seven days and is appropriate for samples and time-sensitive shipments.

**Freight forwarder:** Use a freight forwarder for end-to-end logistics coordination. A good freight forwarder handles export customs clearance in China, marine transit insurance, arrival customs clearance in Australia, and delivery to your warehouse or business. Freight forwarders typically charge a all-in rate that includes their fee, port charges, and documentation costs.

**Customs clearance:** Your freight forwarder typically handles customs clearance as part of their service. They will need your import declaration, commercial invoice, packing list, bill of lading or sea waybill, and Certificate of Origin if you are claiming ChAFTA preferential rates. Your customs broker (if you engaged one separately) may handle the import declaration directly.

## FAQ

**How long does sea freight take from China to Australia?**

Sea freight from major Chinese ports to Australian ports typically takes three to four weeks. The specific transit time depends on the departure port, the arrival port, and whether the vessel makes direct port calls or tranships. Air freight takes three to seven days.

**Do I need a licence to import from China?**

Most products do not require a specific import licence for Australia. However, certain product categories require permits or compliance with Australian standards before they can be imported: food products, cosmetics, medical devices, therapeutic goods, chemicals, and certain electronic products. Check the specific requirements for your product category before ordering. If your product requires mandatory certification (such as electrical safety standards for consumer electronics), ensure your Chinese supplier can provide the required documentation before placing your order.

**What is the customs duty on goods from China?**

Duty rates vary by product category and tariff classification. Many products benefit from zero duty under ChAFTA. Others carry standard MFN rates of 5 to 10 percent or higher. The exact rate for your product is determined by its HS code classification. Confirm the applicable rate before comparing prices — a factory that appears cheaper may not remain so after duty is calculated.

**Can I import from China without visiting a factory?**

Yes. Many Australian businesses source from China without travelling there. However, importing without a factory visit carries risk that can be managed but not eliminated. The mitigation approach is rigorous document verification, physical sample approval, production monitoring, and mandatory pre-shipment inspection. We recommend a factory visit before placing your first substantial order — it changes the supplier relationship fundamentally.

**What is the minimum order quantity for imports from China?**

Minimum order quantities vary by factory and product category. In our experience, typical MOQs for direct factory orders start at 300 to 500 units for most product categories. Some factories — particularly in specialised electronics, industrial components, and certain apparel categories — require minimum orders of 1,000 to 2,000 units on initial orders. We regularly negotiate first-order MOQ reductions for clients by framing the initial order as the beginning of a relationship rather than a one-off transaction.

**What documentation is required for customs clearance in Australia?**

Australian customs clearance typically requires: a commercial invoice with full product description, HS codes, and unit prices; a packing list specifying carton counts, dimensions, and gross weight; a bill of lading (sea freight) or air waybill (air freight); a Certificate of Origin if claiming ChAFTA preferential duty rates; and any product-specific permits, licences, or conformity certificates required for your product category.

**How do I claim ChAFTA duty reductions?**

To claim ChAFTA preferential duty rates, your Chinese supplier must provide a Certificate of Origin confirming that the goods meet the applicable rules of origin under the agreement. The rules of origin specify the percentage of Chinese content or the manufacturing processes that must occur in China for the preferential rate to apply. Not all products qualify for preferential rates — it depends on the product and the manufacturing process. Confirm eligibility with your supplier before relying on ChAFTA for pricing calculations.

**What are the quarantine requirements for wooden packaging from China?**

All wooden packaging material (pallets, crates, dunnage) used in sea freight from China must comply with ISPM 15 standards. This means the wood must have been heat treated or fumigated with methyl bromide and marked with the official ISPM 15 mark. Most reputable Chinese freight forwarders and factories are familiar with this requirement. Using non-compliant wooden packaging will result in goods being held or refused entry at the Australian port.

**How do I handle a quality dispute with a Chinese supplier?**

Quality disputes are resolved most effectively when they are prevented — through sample approval before production, production monitoring during manufacturing, and pre-shipment inspection before shipment. If a quality problem is discovered after goods arrive in Australia, document it thoroughly with photographs and written specifications. Attempt direct communication with the supplier first. For significant amounts, engage a bilingual intermediary or initiate formal dispute resolution as specified in your purchase agreement.

**Should I use a trading company or go direct to a factory?**

For most Australian businesses, direct factory relationships produce better outcomes: lower prices, clearer communication, stronger quality control, and more direct accountability when something goes wrong. Trading companies are appropriate for first imports where you lack the verification capability to go direct, for small orders where the MOQ from a direct factory is too high, or for product categories where you need rapid access to multiple suppliers without managing multiple direct relationships. For any substantial, ongoing import program, direct factory relationships are the correct approach.
