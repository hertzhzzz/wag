During periods of medical supply shortage, the documentation fraud rate in PPE supply chains increases measurably. Australian healthcare businesses receiving supplier certificates during the COVID-19 pandemic and subsequent periods of supply pressure faced fraudulent documentation rates that would have been extraordinary under normal conditions. For a Sydney allied health clinic group, the experience of receiving the same TGA certificate from three different suppliers became the motivation for engaging professional verification before committing to a 50,000-unit procurement.

## The Client

An allied health clinic group operating three locations across Sydney NSW required medical-grade PPE — specifically N95 respirators and protective gowns — for clinical staff across all sites. The order comprised 50,000 units, representing a significant procurement investment for a multi-site allied health operation. The clinic group had been approached by multiple suppliers claiming to have TGA-registered medical PPE available for immediate dispatch.

The procurement team had attempted to verify supplier claims independently by reviewing TGA registration certificates and cross-referencing against the TGA's public Australian Register of Therapeutic Goods (ARTG) database. The cross-referencing identified significant discrepancies — certificate numbers that could not be found, registration holder names that did not match the supplier company, and expiration dates that appeared incorrect. Without regulatory expertise, the procurement team could not resolve the discrepancies and faced the real risk of importing non-compliant PPE that could not legally be used in clinical settings.

## The Challenge

The core challenge was systemic supplier documentation fraud — not individual misrepresentation, but coordinated fraudulent documentation in the PPE supply chain:

**Fraudulent certificate prevalence.** WAG's field experience suggests that during periods of elevated PPE demand, 50–70% of suppliers presenting documentation may be presenting fraudulent, expired, or borrowed certificates. "We have seen the same certificate number presented by different supplier entities across multiple engagements," notes Andy Liu. "In one documented case, a single TGA registration number was presented by five different suppliers claiming to be the registered ARTG entry holder."

**Certificate verification complexity.** TGA ARTG registration verification requires more than database cross-referencing — it requires understanding the relationship between the ARTG entry number, the specific product variant codes, the registration holder entity name, and the Australian Sponsor company. A certificate can be technically valid (a real ARTG number) but not applicable to the specific product being offered (a different product variant, registered by a different entity, under a different Australian Sponsor).

**Documentation chain requirements.** Even with a genuinely TGA-registered product, the import documentation must align precisely with the ARTG entry details — product variant codes, registration holder, Australian Sponsor details, and conformity assessment documentation must all be consistent. Inconsistency at any point in the documentation chain creates customs detention risk.

The Sydney clinic group's procurement team had correctly identified that discrepancies existed but lacked the TGA regulatory knowledge to assess which suppliers were fraudulent and which might be presenting legitimate documentation with clerical errors.

## How WAG Helped

### Step 1: ARTG Database Verification in Guangzhou

WAG obtained copies of TGA registration certificates from six suppliers claiming to offer compliant medical PPE. Rather than accepting these documents at face value, WAG's team cross-referenced each certificate number against the TGA's public ARTG database — directly, in Guangzhou, with access to both the database and the physical documentation simultaneously.

This verification process identified that four of the six suppliers were presenting fraudulent registration documents:

- Two presented expired certificates — registrations that had lapsed and were not renewed
- One presented a certificate number that did not exist in the ARTG registry
- One presented a certificate borrowed from a different supplier's legitimate registration

One particularly concerning finding: the same certificate document was presented by three different suppliers, suggesting a coordinated documentation fraud ring. "Three suppliers sent identical PDF files with the same certificate number," reports Mark He. "The certificate itself was real — but it belonged to a fourth supplier entity. The fraud was not in fabricating the certificate, but in borrowing someone else's legitimate documentation."

Only two suppliers progressed with genuine documentation to detailed verification.

### Step 2: On-Site Facility Audit

With compliant candidates narrowed to two legitimate suppliers, WAG arranged an on-site facility audit of the most promising option in Guangzhou's Medical Equipment Zone. The audit verified:

- Facility registration status against ARTG entry holder documentation
- Production line conditions and quality management system implementation
- Current Good Manufacturing Practice (cGMP) compliance documentation
- Specific product variants' production scope against ARTG registration
- Australian Sponsor documentation alignment

The facility audit confirmed that the selected supplier held genuine TGA registration for the specific N95 respirator and protective gown product variants being offered. The audit also identified that the facility's production capacity was adequate for the 50,000-unit order within the required timeline.

### Step 3: TGA Documentation Coordination

With a verified genuine supplier confirmed, WAG coordinated the TGA documentation preparation process in Guangzhou with an Australian sponsor company — a legal requirement for importing medical devices into Australia, as the sponsor company bears regulatory responsibility for the device's ongoing compliance.

The coordination covered:

- Ensuring all ARTG registration details matched the specific product variants ordered (variant code alignment, product description matching, intended use alignment)
- Preparing the documentation package for TGA submission, including Australian Sponsor authorisation
- Coordinating Australian Customs documentation requirements
- Confirming the import permit was in place before shipment authorisation

## The Results

- **50,000 units of TGA-compliant medical PPE imported** across 3 clinic locations — all genuinely registered under the ARTG
- **$167,000 saved** compared to pricing from non-verified suppliers — approximately 40% cost reduction
- **TGA approval obtained in 11 weeks** from initial inquiry — accelerated timeline achieved through pre-verified documentation
- **Zero documentation issues** at Australian Customs — all documentation aligned with ARTG entry details
- **All PPE accepted for clinical use** with full regulatory compliance — no customs detention, no compliance holds

## Why This Matters for Australian Healthcare Businesses

Medical device and PPE imports to Australia require TGA registration as a legal prerequisite for clinical use. Under the Therapeutic Goods Act 1989, it is an offence to import a medical device that is not included on the ARTG, and the device will be detained by Australian Customs if documentation does not match the ARTG entry.

The fraudulent certificate pattern WAG identified reflects a systemic risk in PPE supply chains during periods of elevated demand. The consequences of importing non-compliant PPE are not merely commercial — they include clinical risk to staff and patients, regulatory liability for the clinic group, and potential criminal liability for individuals who knowingly import non-compliant medical devices.

For Australian allied health businesses, the lesson is direct: supplier claims about TGA registration must be verifiable against the public record, and verification must be conducted by someone with TGA regulatory knowledge, not merely database access. The certificate copy is not the evidence — the database cross-reference is the evidence.

## FAQ

**Q: How do I verify TGA ARTG registration for a Chinese medical device supplier?**
TGA ARTG registration verification requires: (1) obtaining the ARTG entry number and/or the certificate number from the supplier; (2) cross-referencing against the TGA's public ARTG search database (available at tga.gov.au); (3) verifying that the registration holder entity name matches the supplier's business registration; (4) confirming the specific product variant code is included in the registration scope; and (5) confirming the registration is current (not expired). Certificate copies provided by suppliers are not evidence — the database cross-reference is the evidence.

**Q: What is the TGA pathway for importing medical devices to Australia?**
Medical device import requires: an Australian Sponsor company (the legal entity responsible for the device's ongoing compliance), ARTG inclusion (the device must be entered in the ARTG before import), conformity assessment documentation (demonstrating the device meets applicable standards), and for some device classes, TGAConformity Assessment certification. The import permit application is submitted by the Australian Sponsor after verifying the foreign manufacturer's registration status. WAG coordinates the Guangzhou-side documentation; the Australian Sponsor manages TGA submissions.

**Q: What are the consequences of importing non-compliant PPE?**
Non-compliant PPE may be detained by Australian Customs, requiring re-export or destruction at the importer's cost. If the non-compliance is discovered after distribution (i.e., PPE used in clinical settings), the consequences include: regulatory investigation, potential criminal liability under the Therapeutic Goods Act 1989, clinical risk liability if staff or patients are harmed, and reputational damage. Healthcare businesses cannot claim they were misled by suppliers once the documentation chain has been examined — due diligence is the standard.

**Q: What documentation is required for importing medical PPE to Australia?**
Required documentation includes: TGA ARTG registration certificate, Australian Sponsor authorisation letter, conformity assessment documentation, batch-specific Certificate of Analysis (COA), Australian Customs entry documentation, and for some device types, clinical evidence of safety and performance. All documentation must align precisely with the ARTG entry details — product variant codes, registration holder, and sponsor details must be consistent across all documents.

**Q: How long does TGA-compliant PPE import take from China?**
From supplier verification to Sydney delivery, TGA-compliant PPE import typically requires 10–14 weeks: TGA registration verification (1–2 weeks), supplier audit and selection (2–3 weeks), documentation coordination and sponsor submission (3–4 weeks), production (2–4 weeks), and shipping (2–3 weeks via air freight for urgent orders, 4–6 weeks via sea freight). The Sydney clinic group's 11-week timeline was achievable because the selected supplier had existing TGA documentation infrastructure.
