China remains the world's largest garment manufacturer, producing everything from basic t-shirts to high-end designer collections. For Australian fashion brands and clothing retailers sourcing from China, understanding how to verify a garment factory's capabilities is essential.

This guide covers the key manufacturing hubs, what to verify specific to apparel production, and how to manage the sourcing process.

## Key Manufacturing Hubs

### Guangzhou — Casual and Sportswear

Guangzhou's garment district is known for casual wear, sportswear, and fast-fashion production. The Panyu and Baiyun districts host thousands of factories specialising in:
- T-shirts and tops
- Sportswear and activewear
- Denim and casual trousers
- Light outerwear

### Dongguan — Higher-Quality Manufacturing

Dongguan is where the better-quality factories concentrate — those producing for premium brands and export markets with higher quality specifications. Factories here tend to have:
- Better-trained sewing operators
- More rigorous QC systems
- Experience with stricter compliance requirements

### Shanghai Region — Premium and Designer

Shanghai-area factories cater to higher-end fashion brands and international luxury labels. These factories typically:
- Work with finer fabrics and more complex constructions
- Have lower MOQs (500-1000 units per style)
- Charge higher prices but deliver superior quality

## What to Verify in an Apparel Factory

### Production Capacity and Scale

**First, understand whether the factory matches your order size:**

- "What is your monthly production capacity?" (Confirm it exceeds your order volume)
- "How many operators do you have on the production floor?" (Worker count vs claimed output)
- "How many lines can you run simultaneously?" (For larger orders)
- "What is your current production schedule?" (Are they already at capacity?)

<Tip>
A factory claiming 50,000 units monthly capacity but showing 20 workers on the floor is either misrepresenting itself or subcontracting heavily. Both are red flags.
</Tip>

### Fabric Verification

Fabric quality determines the majority of garment quality. This is where most problems originate.

**Ask to verify:**
- Fabric composition (what percentage cotton, polyester, etc.?)
- Fabric weight (gsm — grams per square metre)
- Fabric source (domestic Chinese or imported?)
- Pre-production testing (colour fastness, shrinkage, pilling resistance)

**Request fabric specifications before production:**
- Mill certificate or test report
- Composition breakdown
- Weight and construction details
- Care label requirements

### Construction Quality

Garment construction quality is visible in the details:

- **Stitching** — should be even, consistent, no loose threads; 12-14 stitches per inch is standard for dress shirts
- **Seam reinforcement** — stress points (crotch, collar, armholes) should have additional reinforcement
- **Button attachment** — buttons should be securely attached with sufficient stitches to prevent loosening
- **Finished measurements** — ask to see the measurement tolerance sheet; garments should be within +/- 1cm of spec

### QC Systems

**Ask specifically:**
- "What percentage of garments are inspected at final QC?" (Should be 100% for quality factories)
- "What is your defect tolerance?" (Industry standard is typically 2-4% AQL)
- "Do you have in-house QC staff or external inspection?" (In-house is generally better)
- "Can I arrange a third-party inspection before shipment?" (Standard practice; any reputable factory will accept)

## Minimum Order Quantities

MOQs in Chinese garment manufacturing vary by factory type:

| Factory Type | Typical MOQ per Style |
|-------------|----------------------|
| Basic factory | 1,000-3,000 units |
| Mid-range factory | 500-1,000 units |
| Premium/designer factory | 200-500 units |
| High-end boutique | 50-200 units |

**Factors that affect MOQ:**
- Fabric quantity (fabric minimums apply)
- Complexity of construction
- Number of sizes and colours
- Custom labelling or packaging

<Tip>
Negotiating MOQs is standard practice. Many factories will agree to lower MOQs in exchange for a higher per-unit price or a commitment to larger future orders.
</Tip>

## Australian Compliance Requirements

Australian requirements for imported apparel include:

- **Textile labelling** — fibre content, care instructions in English
- **Size labelling** — must use Australian standard sizes
- **Country of origin** — mandatory on label
- **Fire hazards** — for children's sleepwear, specific fire safety standards apply

Request the factory's experience with Australian labelling requirements before placing your first order. Most factories that export to the US or EU will be familiar with the Australian requirements, but confirm explicitly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I visit factories before ordering?

Yes — visiting before your first order is strongly recommended. Most established factories will accommodate a visit. Use the visit to assess their quality standards, production capacity, and communication before committing to production.

### What is the typical lead time for garment production?

From order confirmation to shipment: 30-45 days for basic styles, 45-60 days for more complex constructions. Add 7-10 days if fabric needs to be sourced specifically for your order. Build in buffer time for sample approval if doing custom development.

### Should I use a sourcing agent for apparel?

For your first few orders, a sourcing agent with apparel industry experience can be valuable — they understand the right factories for your quality level, can manage production follow-up in Chinese, and can arrange quality inspections. Choose an agent with specific apparel experience; general sourcing agents may not have the technical knowledge to verify construction quality.
