Machinery sourcing from China is different from consumer goods. The purchase amounts are larger, the technical complexity is higher, and the relationship with the supplier extends well beyond the initial shipment. For Australian businesses importing industrial machinery from China, thorough on-ground verification is essential.

This guide covers what to verify in a machinery factory, how to assess quality, and what Australian compliance requirements apply.

## Key Machinery Manufacturing Regions

### Pearl River Delta — General Manufacturing Equipment

Guangzhou, Shenzhen, and Dongguan host a wide range of manufacturing equipment factories:
- CNC machine tools
- Industrial automation equipment
- Packaging machinery
- Printing and labelling equipment
- Plastic injection moulding machines

### Yangtze Delta (Shanghai, Jiangsu, Zhejiang) — Precision and Heavy Industry

The Shanghai region produces higher-precision machinery and heavy industrial equipment:
- Precision machining centres
- Industrial robots
- Textile machinery
- Agricultural machinery
- Mining and construction equipment

### Beijing-Tianjin Region — Heavy Industry and Aerospace

For heavier industrial equipment and government-standard manufacturing:
- Heavy machinery
- Industrial heating equipment
- Large-scale production lines

## What to Verify in a Machinery Factory

<FloatImage src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1581091226825-a6a2a5aee158?w=800&q=80" alt="Industrial factory floor with heavy machinery and manufacturing equipment" align="right" width={280} />

### Manufacturing Capability

Before any discussion of the product, verify the factory can actually manufacture what they claim:

- **Production floor inspection** — walk the production floor; are machines actively running?
- **Worker skill levels** — for precision machinery, operator skill matters significantly
- **Quality management system** — ISO 9001 certification indicates a structured quality approach
- **Machine inventory** — do they have the equipment to manufacture the machinery they are selling?

### Performance Data

Never purchase machinery on specifications alone. Request evidence:

- **Performance testing results** — the factory should have test data from the machines they produce
- **Running machines** — ask to see the equipment in operation, not just static demonstration
- **Reference customers** — ask for contact details of previous buyers, especially in your industry
- **Production samples** — can the machine produce a sample output using your materials?

<Tip>
Bring a technical specification sheet with the tolerances that matter for your application. Ask the factory to run a test using your material and measure the output against your spec. This is the only way to verify the machine actually performs as claimed.
</Tip>

### Component Quality

Machinery quality is only as good as the components inside it. Ask specifically:

- **Who supplies the key components?** (Motors, drives, PLC controllers, sensors)
- **Do they use established international brands or in-house/domestic components?**
- **What is the failure rate of key components?**
- **Can you get a component list with brand names before purchase?**

## Safety and Compliance

<FloatImage src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1565793298595-6a879b1d9492?w=800&q=80" alt="Quality control inspection in a manufacturing facility" align="left" width={280} />

### Australian Requirements

Machinery imported into Australia must comply with:
- **Safe Work Australia** — workplace health and safety requirements
- **AS/NZS standards** — specific standards apply to different machine types
- **Electrical safety** — must meet Australian electrical standards (some modifications may be required)
- **CE marking** — not automatically accepted in Australia; verify against specific Australian standards

**Key question to ask:**
"Can you provide documentation showing this machine meets Australian electrical and safety standards, or can you specify what modifications would be required for Australian compliance?"

Any factory experienced with Australian exports will know the answer immediately.

### Documentation to Request

- Operating manual in English
- Electrical schematics
- Safety circuit diagrams
- Maintenance procedures
- Spare parts list with part numbers
- Declaration of conformity

## After-Sales Support

<FloatImage src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1510915361894-db8b601b2d87?w=800&q=80" alt="Modern industrial facility building exterior" align="right" width={280} />

This is where many machinery purchases go wrong. Before purchase, clarify:

- **Warranty terms** — duration, what is covered, who pays for shipping on warranty claims
- **Spare parts availability** — how quickly can parts be supplied? Can they be shipped internationally?
- **Technical support** — is there English-speaking technical support? What are the support hours?
- **Installation and commissioning** — does the factory offer installation services, or just shipping?
- **Remote support capability** — can they diagnose faults via video or remote connection?

<Tip>
Get the name and contact details of the factory's after-sales manager before you leave. If they do not have a dedicated after-sales function, that is a significant risk for a machinery purchase.
</Tip>

## Red Flags in Machinery Sourcing

| Red Flag | What It Signals |
|----------|----------------|
| No test data available | Machine has not been validated |
| Refuses to show production floor | Not the actual manufacturer |
| No after-sales support function | Post-purchase risk |
| Cannot name key component suppliers | Component quality unknown |
| Price significantly below market | Quality compromise or false representation |
| No export experience to Australia | Unfamiliar with compliance requirements |

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What MOQs apply to machinery?

Unlike consumer goods, machinery is typically sold as single units. However, most factories will offer better pricing for 2+ units. For highly customized machinery, the first unit may require significant setup time that makes it relatively expensive; subsequent units of the same specification become cheaper.

### How long does machinery production take?

Lead times vary widely: off-the-shelf equipment may ship within 2-4 weeks. Custom-built or complex machinery can take 3-6 months. Build this into your project timeline and do not commit to tight delivery schedules before confirming with the factory.

### Should I arrange an independent inspection?

For machinery purchases above AUD 50,000, an independent inspection by a third-party engineering company is strongly recommended. This is different from consumer goods inspection — you need someone with specific machinery expertise who can verify mechanical performance, electrical safety, and compliance documentation.
