## Introduction

Most Australian businesses discover the truth about a Chinese supplier too late — after a deposit is paid, after containers have shipped, after quality problems surface in their warehouse.

A factory visit cannot eliminate every risk. But it catches more than any email exchange, video call, or document review ever will.

This checklist covers the complete process: before you book anything, before you board the plane, at the factory, and after you get home. It is the same framework we use for every factory visit we arrange.

If you are planning your first trip to visit factories in China, read this guide before you do anything else.

## Part 1: Before You Book Anything

### 1.1 Why You Cannot Rely on Online Communication Alone

Suppliers in China have become sophisticated at presenting themselves online. A professional website, a Mandarin business card, and a polished Alibaba storefront can all exist for a company that has never manufactured anything.

The warning signs are rarely visible in documents. They are visible in person.

We have visited factories where the address on the business license pointed to an office building — not a production facility. We have visited factories where the equipment was rented for the day and returned before the afternoon shift. We have visited factories where the owner was surprised to see us, because they had been brokering orders from other manufacturers for years.

Nothing replaces standing on the factory floor.

### 1.2 Verify the Supplier Before You Travel

Do not book flights until you have completed the following steps:

**Step 1 — Check the business license against SAMR**

The State Administration for Market Regulation (SAMR) maintains a public registry of all registered companies in China. Search by company name or registration number (统一社会信用代码). Confirm:
- The legal name matches what the supplier has told you
- The registered address corresponds to an actual manufacturing facility
- The business scope includes your product category

**Step 2 — Confirm the factory address with satellite imagery**

Use Google Maps or Baidu Maps to view the registered address. Look for industrial zones, not downtown commercial districts. A factory that lists a residential address should raise immediate questions.

**Step 3 — Cross-reference certifications**

If the supplier claims ISO 9001, CE, or other certifications, verify the certificate numbers directly on the issuing body's website. Certifications can be expired, counterfeit, or issued to a different company with a similar name.

**Step 4 — Request references from Australian or international customers**

A legitimate factory with international experience should be able to provide references from buyers in your industry. Ask for companies in your country or region.

**Step 5 — Conduct a pre-visit audit by a local agent**

If you cannot travel immediately, hire a local inspection agent in China to visit the facility before you commit to a trip. A half-day inspection report costs AUD 200–400 and can save far more than that in wasted travel costs.

**Step 6 — Define your red lines**

Before you engage any supplier, decide what conditions would cause you to walk away. Write them down. Red lines we commonly see businesses set: refusal to provide business license copies, inability to verify the address, a quote that is significantly lower than market rate.

### 1.3 When to Sign an NDA

If you are sharing product specifications, formulations, or proprietary designs with a supplier before visiting, request a Non-Disclosure Agreement. Chinese law recognises NDAs, and reputable manufacturers are accustomed to signing them.

Do not share full technical specifications before you have a signed NDA and confirmed the factory is a genuine manufacturer.

## Part 2: Planning Your China Trip

### 2.1 Visa and Passport Requirements for Australian Citizens

Australian passport holders can enter mainland China with:
- **Tourist visa (L visa)** — valid for stays up to 30 days, suitable for short visits
- **Business visa (M or F visa)** — required if you will sign contracts, attend meetings, or conduct commercial activities
- **Visa-free transit** — some Chinese cities offer 72-hour or 144-hour visa-free transit for international travellers, but this is not reliable for business purposes

Apply for your business visa at the Chinese Visa Application Centre in Australia. Processing takes 5–10 business days. You will need:
- Passport valid for 6+ months beyond your intended departure date
- Invitation letter from your Chinese contact or sponsor
- Completed visa application form
- Passport-sized photograph

Allow 4–6 weeks total lead time.

### 2.2 Which City to Visit

The right manufacturing hub depends on your product category:

**Shenzhen and Dongguan (Pearl River Delta)**

Best for: Electronics, consumer tech, AV equipment, smart devices, printing and packaging, plastics and moulding.

Why: The world's most concentrated electronics manufacturing cluster. Component suppliers, assembly plants, and testing facilities are all within a two-hour drive. The supply chain density means lower tooling costs and faster prototyping.

**Guangzhou (Pearl River Delta)**

Best for: Furniture, garments, textiles, leather goods, machinery.

Why: historic manufacturing base with established export infrastructure. Large wholesale markets and trading companies alongside genuine factories.

**Hangzhou and Ningbo (Zhejiang)**

Best for: E-commerce products, homewares, textiles, machinery, solar equipment.

Why: Strong logistics infrastructure and proximity to the port of Ningbo, one of China's busiest container ports.

**Shanghai and Suzhou (Yangtze River Delta)**

Best for: Automotive components, aerospace parts, precision engineering, chemicals, cosmetics.

Why: Higher-specification manufacturing with stronger quality control cultures. Higher labour costs but more consistent output.

**Yiwu (Zhejiang)**

Best for: General consumer goods, wholesale purchasing, novelties, packaging.

Why: The world's largest small commodity market. Ideal for businesses that want to compare many suppliers in one city. Less suitable for bespoke manufacturing.

### 2.3 Flights, Accommodation, and Local Transport

**Flights**

- Shanghai Pudong (PVG) and Guangzhou Baiyun (CAN) are the most common entry points for manufacturing visits
- Shenzhen Bao'an (SZX) is convenient for Pearl River Delta visits but fewer international routes
- Fly into Shanghai if your suppliers are in Zhejiang or Jiangsu; fly into Guangzhou or Shenzhen if they are in Guangdong

**Accommodation**

Book a hotel in the district where your suppliers are located. In Shenzhen, the Futian and Nanshan districts are central and well-served by metro. In Guangzhou, the Tianhe district near the Canton Fair complex is convenient.

Budget AUD 120–250 per night for a business-class hotel. Do not stay in a resort or airport hotel — you will waste hours commuting.

**Local Transport**

Use Didi (Chinese version of Uber) for all ground transport. It is reliable, inexpensive, and works with foreign credit cards if you link them to the app before you arrive. Download and set up your Didi account before you leave Australia.

For inter-city travel between manufacturing hubs, the high-speed rail network (Dongfang China Rail) is faster and more reliable than domestic flights for journeys under 5 hours.

### 2.5 Cultural Etiquette: What Australian Businesses Get Wrong

**Do not**

- Present clocks or watches as gifts — in Chinese culture this symbolises death
- Give sharp objects (knives, scissors) — symbolises cutting the relationship
- Use the number 4 in any context — it sounds like the word for death
- Shake hands and then immediately put your hands in your pockets — considered rude
- Accept tea with one hand — use both hands or support your right arm with your left hand

**Do**

- Exchange business cards with both hands, read the card carefully before putting it away
- Accept tea when offered — refusing tea can be perceived as rejecting hospitality
- Arrive on time or slightly early — punctuality is respected
- Allow your host to introduce you to others before you introduce yourself
- Reciprocate hospitality — if a supplier takes you to dinner, you should offer to pay for the next meal

## Part 3: At the Factory

### 3.1 Documents to Request Immediately

When you arrive, ask for the following documents before anything else:

1. **Business License (营业执照)** — the primary registration document for any Chinese company. Verify the registration number against SAMR on your phone while you are at the factory.
2. **Quality Certifications** — ISO 9001, CE, CB, or any industry-specific certifications. Photograph the certificate and verify the number on the issuing body's website.
3. **Export License** — if the factory will be exporting directly to you, they need an export license. Ask to see it.
4. **Previous Export Records** — ask for shipping documents from recent orders to the same region or country. This confirms they have experience with your target market.

### 3.2 What to Observe in the Facility

Walk the entire facility. Do not follow only the path the factory wants to show you.

**Production floor**

- Are machines operational? A factory with all equipment turned off during a scheduled visit is either not actively producing or not the primary manufacturer.
- Is the production scale consistent with what you were quoted? If they claim to produce 10,000 units per month but the floor shows capacity for 1,000, there is a mismatch.
- Is the facility organised? Clean facilities generally indicate better quality management.
- Are workers using correct PPE? Lack of basic safety measures suggests broader management problems.

**Storage and packaging areas**

- Are finished goods clearly labelled and separated by order?
- Is there a dedicated quality control area with testing equipment?
- Are raw materials stored properly, with batch numbers and dates?

**Office areas**

- Is there a dedicated R&D or engineering team on-site?
- Do they have the testing equipment your product requires?

### 3.3 Questions You Must Ask on the Factory Floor

Ask these questions at every factory visit, regardless of what you were told in emails:

1. What is your minimum order quantity (MOQ) and why?
2. What is the typical lead time from deposit to shipment?
3. What is included in the tooling or setup costs — and what are the conditions for refunding them?
4. Who handles quality control — in-house or third party?
5. What percentage of orders have you shipped to Australia or New Zealand in the past 12 months?
6. Can I see your testing equipment for [your specific product requirements]?
7. What happens if the products do not meet agreed specifications?
8. Who is your most recent international customer, and can I contact them?
9. Do you manufacture this product yourself or subcontract any part of production?
10. What are your payment terms for international buyers?

If the factory cannot clearly answer questions 4, 9, or 10, treat this as a serious warning.

### 3.4 Red Flags That Should Stop You from Signing

These signs have preceded problems in the majority of cases we have reviewed:

- **The factory refuses to let you photograph the production line.** Legitimate manufacturers do not hide their operations.
- **The equipment on the floor does not match the claimed production capacity.** Machines may be borrowed or rented for the day.
- **The sample products look different from what you were shown in photos.** Always ask to see samples produced from the actual production line.
- **There is no visible quality control process.** No testing stations, no inspection records, no QC staff on the floor.
- **The factory rushes you to sign a contract before you leave.** Legitimate factories are comfortable with due diligence.
- **The owner cannot explain the production process step by step.** If they cannot walk you through their own process, they may be brokering from other facilities.
- **The quoted price is significantly below market rate.** Chinese manufacturers have thin margins. A price that seems too good to be true almost always is.

### 3.5 Photography and Documentation

Take photographs and videos throughout the visit. The best evidence is:

- The production line in operation
- The specific machines being used for your product
- Batch numbers and labels on finished goods
- The quality control area and any testing equipment
- The loading docks and packaging area
- Nameplates on key equipment showing manufacturer and model

Photograph all documents the factory provides. Date and timestamp your photos.

## Part 4: After the Visit

### 4.1 Review Your Notes Within 48 Hours

While the visit is fresh, write up a structured comparison of the factories you visited. Include:

- What you confirmed as legitimate
- What raised questions
- Specific red flags
- Terms quoted verbally
- Your overall impression of management quality

### 4.2 Request a Formal Price Quotation

After the visit, request a formal quotation that includes:

- Unit price by order quantity break points
- Tooling and setup costs (and conditions for refund if order proceeds)
- Payment terms — typical for international buyers is 30% deposit, 70% balance before shipment
- Lead time from deposit to ready-for-shipment
- Port of loading and estimated shipping time to Australia
- All relevant Incoterms (typically FOB, CIF, or DDP for Australian buyers)

Do not proceed without a formal written quotation that includes all of the above.

### 4.3 Negotiating Payment Terms

Standard international payment terms for first orders from China:

| Order stage | Payment |
|-------------|---------|
| Deposit (after contract signed) | 30% of total order value |
| Balance (before shipment) | 70% of total order value |
| Repeat orders | May qualify for improved terms (L/C or open account) |

Never pay 100% upfront on a first order. A deposit of 30% is standard and protects both parties. If a factory insists on full payment before shipment, this is a significant risk signal.

### 4.4 Quality Control After the Visit

Even after a successful visit, arrange independent quality control before shipment:

- **Pre-shipment inspection (PSI)** — a third-party inspector visits the factory before container loading and checks product quality, quantity, and packaging against your purchase order. Typically AUD 200–400 per day for an experienced inspector in China.
- **Container loading supervision** — an inspector attends when the container is loaded to confirm correct goods are packed and secured properly.
- **Testing samples** — request pre-shipment samples and test them against your specifications before the balance is paid.

## FAQ

**Do Australian businesses need a visa to visit factories in China?**

Yes. Australian citizens need a business visa (M or F) or a tourist visa (L) to enter China for business purposes. Visa-free transit options are limited and not reliable for factory visits. Apply 4–6 weeks before your planned travel date.

**How far in advance should I contact a factory before visiting?**

Give factories at least 2–3 weeks notice. Many factories in China will not accept unannounced visitors. The more notice you provide, the more likely the factory will have relevant staff and documentation prepared.

**What is the difference between a factory and a trading company?**

A factory (manufacturer) owns the production equipment and employs workers to make your product. A trading company sources products from factories and resells them to buyers. Trading companies often appear more professional in marketing materials because they invest more in presentation. A factory visit is the most reliable way to distinguish between the two.

**Should I visit more than one factory on the same trip?**

Yes. Visit at least 2–3 factories for the same product before making a decision. Comparing factories in person reveals differences in quality, communication, and professionalism that are invisible in email exchanges. We typically arrange visits to 2–3 pre-screened factories per day.

**What if I cannot travel to China myself?**

You can hire a local inspection agent in China to conduct a pre-visit audit on your behalf. A half-day inspection report typically costs AUD 200–400. This is not a substitute for a personal visit, but it provides more information than documents and photographs alone.

**How do I know if a Chinese factory is legitimate?**

Check the business license against SAMR (China's company registry). Verify quality certifications directly with the issuing body. Confirm the registered address matches the physical location. Conduct a pre-visit audit if you cannot travel. And visit the factory in person before paying any significant deposit.

**What payment terms should I negotiate for my first order?**

The standard for first orders is 30% deposit, 70% balance before shipment. Be cautious of factories that demand full payment upfront. Never pay 100% before you have received and inspected the goods.

**How do I protect myself if something goes wrong after I pay?**

Use a formal purchase agreement with clear specifications, quality requirements, and dispute resolution terms. Arrange pre-shipment inspection by a third party. Use a letter of credit (L/C) for larger orders. Never transfer funds to personal accounts — all payments should go to the company's official bank account.
