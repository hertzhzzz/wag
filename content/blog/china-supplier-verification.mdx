When sourcing from China, the supplier you communicate with may not be the company that actually manufactures your goods. Trading companies, intermediaries, and in extreme cases, fraudulent operators all present themselves as direct manufacturers. Without proper verification, Australian businesses risk losing deposits, receiving substandard products, or discovering too late that they are dealing with shell companies. Supplier verification is not optional for serious importers. It is a fundamental due diligence step that protects your capital, your reputation, and your supply chain. This guide provides a practical framework you can apply to verify Chinese suppliers before committing to orders.

## Why Supplier Verification Matters

The consequences of poor supplier verification extend beyond lost money. Australian importers have legal responsibilities for the products they bring into the country. If you cannot trace your products to a legitimate manufacturer, you may face compliance issues with Australian Consumer Law, product safety standards, and biosecurity requirements.

Working with unverified suppliers creates multiple risks:

- **Financial loss**: Deposits paid to companies that disappear or fail to deliver
- **Quality problems**: Products that do not match specifications because you never verified the actual manufacturer
- **Compliance failures**: Goods that cannot be sold in Australia because they do not meet required standards
- **Supply disruption**: Discovering mid-production that your supplier cannot meet volume or quality requirements

<Tip>
Always verify suppliers before paying any deposit. The cost of verification is trivial compared to the potential loss of a large order or the legal consequences of importing non-compliant products.
</Tip>

## Step 1: Check the Business License and Company Registry

Every legitimate Chinese company has a business license registered with local authorities. This document contains critical information: the company name, unified social credit code, registered address, business scope, and registered capital.

The key detail most importers miss is the business scope. A company with business scope limited to trading, wholesale, or export activities is not a manufacturer. Look for terms like production, manufacturing, processing, or factory in the business scope to confirm the company operates production facilities.

<Tip>
Request a copy of the supplier's business license and verify the details through China's National Enterprise Credit Information Publicity System at gsxt.gov.cn. This official database confirms whether the company exists and provides current registration information.
</Tip>

The unified social credit code is the company's unique identifier. Use this to search the gsxt database and confirm the company is active, has no abnormal operating status, and matches the information provided by the supplier.

## Step 2: Verify the Registered Address

A common deception tactic is listing a manufacturing address that differs from the registered address. Some trading companies rent space in factory districts to pose as manufacturers during buyer visits while operating from different premises.

Compare the address on the business license with the address from which samples ship, the address on invoices, and the address the supplier claims as their factory location.

<Tip>
If the sample ships from Guangdong but the business license shows a registered address in Zhejiang, investigate further. Significant geographic mismatches often indicate the supplier is not the actual manufacturer.
</Tip>

For serious verification, use satellite imagery to compare the claimed factory address with the described operation. Industrial areas in Chinese manufacturing hubs like Shenzhen, Guangzhou, Dongguan, and Ningbo are extensively documented on mapping services.

## Step 3: Analyze Product Range and Minimum Order Quantities

Genuine factories tend to be more specialized. A company claiming to manufacture electronics, textiles, furniture, and plastic products simultaneously is almost certainly a trading company aggregating from multiple manufacturers.

Minimum order quantities (MOQ) provide additional signals. Factories typically have higher MOQs because they operate production lines that require certain volumes to be economically viable. Unusually low MOQs combined with extremely broad catalogs typically indicate a trading intermediary.

<Tip>
If a supplier offers very low MOQs, extremely low prices, and ships from trading hub cities like Yiwu rather than manufacturing centers, these combined signals warrant deeper verification before proceeding.
</Tip>

Ask specific questions about production capabilities: What machinery do you have? How many production lines? What is your monthly capacity? Specific answers indicate real manufacturing experience. Vague or generic responses suggest otherwise.

## Step 4: Request Live Production Evidence

Photos and videos provided by suppliers are easily staged. The most reliable evidence is a live video call showing the actual production facility, active production lines, machinery, workers, and quality control stations.

During a live tour, pay attention to details. Does the facility match the size and type described? Are there signs of active production? Do workers appear to be working on real orders, not just posing for the camera?

<Tip>
Request a live video call during working hours (Monday to Friday, 9am-6pm China time). Ask the supplier to show specific equipment, raw material storage, and current production. Genuine factories accommodate these requests readily.
</Tip>

If a supplier consistently avoids video calls or offers only pre-recorded content, treat this as a significant warning sign.

## Step 5: Verify Certificates with Issuing Authorities

Certificates from ISO, SGS, BV, or other testing organizations add credibility, but they are also commonly misused. Always verify certificates with the issuing organization, not just accept file copies.

Common certificate fraud includes: expired certificates still presented as current, certificates issued to different company names, certificates covering different product categories than what is being offered, and completely fabricated certificates.

<Tip>
Contact the certificate issuer directly using contact information from their official website, not contact details provided by the supplier. Confirm the certificate number, validity, scope, and that it was issued to the company you are negotiating with.
</Tip>

For products destined for Australia, ensure any claimed certifications are recognized in Australia. Not all international certificates meet Australian regulatory requirements.

## Step 6: Conduct a Factory Visit

The most reliable verification method remains an in-person factory visit. Walking through production facilities, observing operations firsthand, and meeting the people responsible for your order provides insights that no document or video call can match.

During a factory visit, assess:

- Production scale and equipment condition
- Worker numbers and employment terms
- Quality control processes and testing equipment
- Sample room and prototype capabilities
- General organization and professionalism
- Willingness to show complete operations

<Tip>
Visit during normal business hours without announced notice when possible. Unexpected visits reveal the true state of operations better than announced inspections where everything can be prepared in advance.
</Tip>

Australian businesses that visit factories before placing significant orders consistently report higher satisfaction rates and fewer supply chain problems.

## Red Flags to Watch For

During supplier evaluation, these warning signs indicate elevated risk:

- Business scope shows only trading, wholesale, or export without manufacturing
- Company name or registered address changes between documents
- Extremely low prices below market rates without clear cost explanation
- Reluctance to provide live video of production facilities
- Certificates with mismatched company names or expired dates
- Vague or inconsistent answers about production capacity
- Pushy sales tactics demanding quick decisions or deposits
- Requests for payment to personal accounts or unusual jurisdictions

<Tip>
No single red flag should necessarily stop you, but multiple red flags together indicate the supplier may not be who they claim to be. Trust your instincts and walk away if something feels wrong.
</Tip>

## What to Verify Before Paying a Deposit

Before sending any payment to a Chinese supplier, confirm these elements:

1. **Business license verified**: Confirmed through gsxt.gov.cn with production in business scope
2. **Address consistency**: Business license, shipping, and invoice addresses align
3. **Certificate validity**: Verified directly with issuing organizations
4. **Company bank account**: Payment account name matches the legal company name
5. **Clear contract terms**: Specifies the actual manufacturer, not vague promises
6. **Sample approval**: Product sample matches your specifications before production begins

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long does supplier verification take?

Initial document verification can be completed in 1-2 business days. Live video verification requests can be arranged within a week. Full factory visit verification typically requires 2-4 weeks including travel arrangements.

### Can I verify suppliers remotely?

Yes. Start with business license and registry checks online, then request live video tours of facilities. For important orders, plan an in-person visit to verify capabilities firsthand. Remote verification reduces but does not eliminate risk.

### What is the difference between a factory and a trading company?

A factory manufactures products in its own facilities. A trading company connects buyers with factories without manufacturing anything itself. Trading companies add cost without adding value, and they often lack control over quality and production timelines.

### Should I use a third-party inspection service?

Third-party inspection services (SGS, Bureau Veritas, QIMA) provide professional verification and can conduct factory audits on your behalf. For significant orders, a professional inspection report provides valuable assurance. However, nothing replaces your own relationship and understanding of the supplier.

### What should I do if I discover my supplier is a trading company?

If you discover you are dealing with a trading company rather than a factory, evaluate whether their services add value. If they provide quality control, logistics, and communication support that justifies their markup, they may still be worth using. If they simply add cost without value, find an alternative source.
