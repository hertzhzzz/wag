Australian Customs does not accept commercial invoices at face value. When a commercial invoice shows a transaction value materially below the fair market value of imported goods, Australian Customs has legal authority to apply alternative valuation methods — and when it does, the penalty assessments can substantially exceed any supposed Customs duty saving from the undervaluation. For an Adelaide workshop equipment supplier, a 6-week customs hold and a potential 25% penalty assessment on the shipment value became the expensive introduction to Australian transfer pricing rules that govern all related-party international transactions.

## The Client

A workshop equipment supplier based in Adelaide SA imports industrial metalworking machinery from Chinese manufacturers for distribution to tool shops, engineering workshops, and manufacturing operations across South Australia. The supplier had been sourcing machinery through a combination of European manufacturers and Australian distributors, with pricing that reflected the overhead of those supply chains. Direct sourcing from Chinese manufacturers offered the potential for 30–50% cost reduction — a commercially significant opportunity for a business with multiple container-level import volumes.

The supplier had previously experienced a customs hold that highlighted the documentation complexity of international machinery imports. A shipment of metalworking equipment had been held at Adelaide port for six weeks due to commercial invoice discrepancies — Australian Customs had determined that the transaction values shown on the supplier's invoices were artificially low relative to the fair market value of the machinery. The supplier faced potential penalty assessments of up to 25% of the shipment value under Australian Customs valuation rules. The hold, the legal fees, and the eventual penalty negotiation consumed resources that exceeded the initial cost saving from the low-value invoicing.

## The Challenge

The core challenge was customs valuation compliance — not merely correct invoicing, but documentation that satisfies Australian Customs and the ATO's transfer pricing requirements for related-party international transactions:

**Australian Customs valuation rules.** Under Part VIII of the Customs Act 1901 and the Customs Valuation Regulations, Australian Customs applies specific rules for determining the customs value of imported goods. For related-party transactions (where the buyer and seller have a relationship), the transaction value is acceptable only if the Customs Valuation Code conditions are met — specifically, that the transaction value is the sole consideration for the sale, and that the relationship has not affected the price. If Customs determines that the transaction value does not reflect an arm's-length price, it may apply alternative valuation methods.

**ATO transfer pricing requirements.** Under the Income Tax Assessment Act 1997 (Division 13 and 815), the Australian Taxation Office requires that related-party international transactions reflect arm's-length pricing. For machinery imports from a related-party Chinese manufacturer, this means the transaction price must be supported by transfer pricing documentation demonstrating that the price is consistent with what unrelated parties would agree to.

**Chinese supplier documentation practices.** Chinese machinery manufacturers frequently prepare commercial invoices for export clearance purposes that reflect the transaction price agreed with the buyer — but this price may be structured to reduce import duty exposure rather than to reflect fair market value. Invoices that show significantly lower values than the machinery's fair market value will trigger Customs valuation review, even if the low value reflects the actual commercial agreement between buyer and seller.

"Chinese suppliers are not trying to commit fraud when they prepare low-value invoices," notes Andy Liu. "They are following practices common in Chinese domestic trade that do not translate to Australian regulatory requirements. Australian Customs is not interested in the supplier's internal pricing structure — it is interested in the transaction value as declared on the commercial invoice, and whether that value reflects what an arm's-length buyer and seller would agree to."

## How WAG Helped

### Step 1: Factory Identification with Documentation Capability Assessment

WAG identified four machinery factories in Dongguan and Shenzhen with established experience producing industrial metalworking equipment for English-speaking export markets. The screening criteria applied:

- Documented track record with Australian Customs clearances (verified through references and import history)
- Documentation preparation capabilities meeting Australian requirements
- Familiarity with Australian import requirements, specifically Customs valuation and biosecurity documentation
- Financial stability and business registration verification

The documentation capability assessment eliminated two factories that had previously prepared invoices for Australian clients that triggered customs valuation reviews — not because they intended fraud, but because they did not understand Australian requirements.

### Step 2: Documentation Preparation Meeting Australian Requirements

WAG prepared commercial invoice documentation meeting Australian Customs requirements for all transactions. The documentation preparation covered:

- **Commercial invoice preparation** with transaction values reflecting actual agreed pricing, structured to satisfy Customs valuation requirements
- **Transfer pricing documentation** review against ATO guidelines for related-party transactions, confirming the transaction price was consistent with arm's-length pricing principles
- **Pre-shipment documentation review** against Customs Act requirements before shipment authorisation

The critical insight was that the transaction value on the commercial invoice must reflect the actual commercial agreement between buyer and seller — but the documentation supporting that value must demonstrate why the price is acceptable under Australian rules. "The solution is not to inflate the invoice to an artificially high value," explains Mark He. "The solution is to document why the actual agreed price is an arm's-length price that satisfies Customs valuation requirements."

### Step 3: Loading Inspection and Biosecurity Documentation

WAG coordinated loading inspection at the Dongguan factory to verify machinery security and packaging compliance. For machinery with wooden packaging, fumigation documentation was arranged to satisfy Australian biosecurity requirements:

- **ISPM 15 compliance** — all wooden packaging materials (pallets, crates, and dunnage) must be heat-treated or fumigated with methyl bromide and marked according to ISPM 15 requirements
- **Phytosanitary certificate** from China's CIQIA confirming treatment and marking compliance
- **Packaging condition inspection** — verifying that machinery was adequately secured for sea freight transport

The loading inspection verified that all documentation was present and accurate before the container was sealed — preventing the documentation gaps that cause customs holds.

## FAQ

**Q: What are Australian Customs valuation rules for machinery imports?**
Australian Customs values imported goods under the Customs Valuation Code (Schedule to the Customs Act 1901). The primary method is transaction value — the price actually paid or payable. For related-party transactions, the transaction value is acceptable if: the price is the sole consideration, the relationship has not affected the price, and the price reflects an arm's-length outcome. If these conditions are not met, Customs may apply alternative valuation methods (computed value, deductive value, or fallback methods) that typically produce higher valuations and additional penalty assessments.

**Q: What transfer pricing documentation is required for related-party machinery imports?**
Under the Income Tax Assessment Act 1997 (Division 13 and 815), related-party international transactions must reflect arm's-length pricing. Transfer pricing documentation should include: nature of the relationship, pricing methodology applied, comparable uncontrolled transaction data supporting the price, and a contemporaneous business record explaining the pricing rationale. The documentation must be prepared at or before the time of the transaction — retrospective documentation is not acceptable to the ATO.

**Q: What ISPM 15 documentation is required for wooden packaging on machinery imports?**
All wooden packaging material (pallets, crates, dunnage) used in sea freight must comply with ISPM 15 (International Standards for Phytosanitary Measures), which requires heat treatment (HT) or dielectric treatment (DB) and marking. Documentation required includes: a phytosanitary certificate issued by China's CIQIA confirming the treatment method and marking, and the marking itself must appear on the wooden material. Australian Biosecurity may inspect wooden packaging on arrival and may require treatment at the import facility if documentation is incomplete.

**Q: What is the typical cost differential for industrial machinery from China versus Australian or European alternatives?**
Our field data from machinery import engagements suggests Chinese-manufactured industrial machinery typically prices 30–50% below Australian distributor equivalents and 40–60% below European equivalents for comparable specifications. For metalworking machinery (lathes, milling machines, CNC equipment), cost reductions of $50,000–$200,000 per unit are achievable at the larger end of the specification range. However, these savings require documentation compliance infrastructure to prevent customs valuation disputes.

**Q: How long does industrial machinery import from China to Adelaide take?**
From factory selection to Adelaide delivery, industrial machinery imports typically require 10–16 weeks: pre-qualification and documentation preparation (2–4 weeks), production (4–8 weeks depending on machinery complexity), pre-shipment inspection (1–2 weeks), and sea freight to Adelaide (3–5 weeks). The Adelaide supplier's first shipment was delivered in 14 weeks.
