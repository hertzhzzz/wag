"Food grade" means something different in China than it does in Australia. The phrase sounds like a universal standard — food-safe, fit for human consumption, compliant with relevant regulations. What it actually means in a Chinese manufacturing context depends entirely on which certification body issued the documentation and which market's standards that certification was evaluated against. For an Adelaide specialty coffee roaster sourcing private label instant coffee through Singapore, the distinction between Chinese domestic "food grade" certification and internationally accredited HACCP certification was the difference between a viable supply chain and a shipment rejection at Singapore customs.

## The Client

A specialty coffee roaster based in Adelaide SA required a private label instant coffee supply for the Southeast Asian market. The roaster had established a specialty coffee brand with strong positioning in Adelaide's inner-suburban specialty coffee scene and was expanding distribution into Singapore through a specialty food importer. The Singapore market offered distribution potential that Australian domestic expansion alone could not match — Singapore's specialty food import market is sophisticated, and private label instant coffee sourced from Yunnan province at Chinese domestic pricing creates meaningful margin opportunity for brand owners.

The client had previously sourced private label instant coffee through a Singapore distributor at landed pricing that reflected the distributor's margin. With Singapore distribution rights secured directly, the opportunity was to source directly from Chinese manufacturers at pricing that excluded the Singapore distributor's margin — while maintaining the food safety documentation that Singapore's regulatory environment demands.

## The Challenge

The client's primary challenge was regulatory navigation — specifically, understanding that Chinese domestic food safety certifications do not automatically satisfy international market requirements:

**Chinese domestic "food grade" vs. internationally accredited HACCP.** Chinese food safety certification operates through a two-tier system: the national food safety standard (GB standards) and voluntary certification programs including HACCP (Hazard Analysis Critical Control Points). Chinese domestic "food grade" marking indicates compliance with GB standards for domestic market sale — it does not indicate the accredited third-party certification that international markets require. Singapore's Singapore Food Agency (SFA, formerly AVA) requirements specifically reference HACCP certification from accredited certification bodies, not Chinese domestic food grade declarations.

"Approximately 70% of Chinese food manufacturers hold some form of food safety certification," notes Andy Liu. "Approximately 20% hold internationally accredited HACCP certification from bodies recognised under the GFSI (Global Food Safety Initiative) benchmark framework. The two categories look similar in documentation — but only one category clears Singapore customs without additional documentation requirements."

**Certification database verification.** One of three shortlisted Yunnan roasters presented HACCP certification documentation that, upon WAG verification through the issuing certification body's database, was found to have lapsed. The certification had expired eight months prior and had not been renewed. Without WAG's direct database verification, the supplier would have appeared compliant based on documentation alone.

**AQSIQ export approval process.** For food products exported from China, the General Administration of Customs of China (GACC, formerly AQSIQ) requires specific export approval documentation. Singapore SFA requires this documentation as a condition of import permit issuance. The AQSIQ approval process requires the manufacturer to be registered for the specific product category — a registration that is product-specific and facility-specific.

## How WAG Helped

### Step 1: Manufacturer Identification and HACCP Verification

WAG identified three coffee roasters in Yunnan province (primarily in Kunming and Dali regions) with documented HACCP certification and export experience to international markets. Shortlisting criteria applied:

- Current HACCP certification from an accredited body recognised under GFSI (SGS, Bureau Veritas, Intertek, or equivalent)
- Production scope covering instant coffee (spray-dried and/or freeze-dried) with private label capability
- Existing export documentation systems for Singapore market
- AQSIQ export registration for the instant coffee product category

WAG verified HACCP certificates directly through issuing certification body databases — not via supplier-provided documentation. This verification identified that one supplier's HACCP certification had lapsed and was therefore ineligible for the engagement.

### Step 2: On-Site Visit and Sensory Evaluation

WAG arranged a two-day on-site visit to the two remaining Yunnan roasters for sensory evaluation and HACCP documentation review. The site visit served multiple purposes:

**Sensory evaluation.** Private label instant coffee quality depends critically on the green bean sourcing, roast profile, and extraction process. For the Adelaide roaster's specialty positioning, flavour profile consistency across production batches was non-negotiable. The site visit allowed the client's head roaster to evaluate current production runs and assess whether the facility could deliver the flavour profile the brand required.

**HACCP documentation audit.** The site visit included a detailed review of HACCP documentation — including hazard analysis records, critical control point monitoring logs, corrective action records, and verification procedures. Documentation review confirmed operational implementation, not just certification status.

**Production capacity and scheduling.** The visit confirmed production capacity for the 2,400kg order within the required timeframe and established the production scheduling communication protocol for future orders.

One roaster was eliminated during the site visit — their HACCP documentation was current and accurate, but their production equipment for freeze-dried instant coffee had maintenance scheduling that would not accommodate the required delivery timeline.

### Step 3: Documentation Coordination for Singapore Import

With the selected roaster confirmed, WAG coordinated all documentation required for Singapore import:

- **Certificate of Analysis (COA)** for each production batch, with full chemical and microbiological test results
- **Health certificates** under Singapore SFA requirements
- **AQSIQ export approval** documentation for the instant coffee product category
- **Private label artwork approval** documentation with Singapore importer sign-off

WAG reviewed all documentation against Singapore SFA import requirements before shipment authorisation. "Documentation coordination is not simply document collection," notes Mark He. "It requires understanding which documentation each regulatory authority requires, in which format, and with which issuing authority's certification. Chinese exporters frequently do not understand Singapore's specific requirements because they have not previously exported to that market. The documentation package must be compiled by someone who understands both Chinese export procedures and Singapore import requirements."

- **Zero documentation issues** at Singapore customs — all documentation accepted at first presentation
- **AQSIQ export approval secured** before first shipment — no delays at Chinese customs
- **Singapore SFA documentation accepted** without queries — the HACCP documentation package satisfied all import requirements

## Why This Matters for Australian Food and Beverage Businesses

The Australian specialty food and beverage market presents a structural opportunity for private label sourcing from Chinese manufacturers — particularly for products where Chinese domestic pricing creates meaningful landed cost advantages after accounting for shipping, duties, and compliance documentation. Singapore's role as a regional food trade hub amplifies this opportunity for Australian brands distributing into Southeast Asia.

The regulatory navigation challenge is the primary barrier. Chinese domestic food safety certifications — including "food grade" declarations, local HACCP certifications, and provincial food safety permits — do not automatically satisfy the documentation requirements of Singapore SFA, Australian DAFF, or other destination market regulators. The certification landscape requires specific knowledge: which certifications are recognised by which markets, how to verify certification validity through independent databases, and how to compile documentation packages that satisfy both Chinese export requirements and destination market import requirements.

For Australian specialty food brands considering private label sourcing from China, the food safety documentation gap is the most common point of failure. "We have seen shipments rejected at Singapore, Malaysia, and Australian ports due to documentation deficiencies that could have been prevented with pre-shipment verification," reports Andy Liu. "The cost of a rejected food shipment — including treatment or destruction costs, re-shipping costs, and relationship damage with distributors — typically exceeds the landed cost advantage of the original shipment."

## FAQ

**Q: What HACCP certification is required for food exports from China to Singapore?**
Singapore SFA requires HACCP certification from certification bodies accredited under the Global Food Safety Initiative (GFSI) — including SGS, Bureau Veritas, Intertek, DNV GL, and Lloyd's Register. Chinese domestic HACCP certifications issued by non-GFSI-accredited bodies are not accepted. Verification should be conducted directly through the issuing certification body's online database before any commitment is made to a supplier.

**Q: What is the AQSIQ/GACC export approval process for Chinese food products?**
The General Administration of Customs of China (GACC) requires food export registration for specific product categories. The registration process requires: facility inspection by GACC, product-specific registration approval, and inclusion in the GACC registry of approved exporters. Singapore SFA requires GACC/AQSIQ export documentation as a condition of import permit issuance. WAG coordinates this process with the selected manufacturer before shipment.

**Q: What documentation is required for importing coffee products into Singapore?**
Singapore SFA import requirements for coffee products include: HACCP certification (GFSI-accredited), COA with full chemical and microbiological test results, health certificate issued by GACC, GACC export registration confirmation, private label approval documentation (if applicable), and import permit from Singapore SFA. All documentation must be in English or accompanied by certified English translations.

**Q: What is the typical MOQ for private label instant coffee from Chinese roasters?**
Private label instant coffee from Chinese roasters typically starts at 500–1,000kg for initial production runs, with many facilities accommodating 1,000–2,000kg for international private label orders. Freeze-dried instant coffee generally has higher minimums than spray-dried due to production equipment batch sizes. For the Adelaide roaster's 2,400kg order, the facility accommodated the order as a single production run with no difficulty.

**Q: How do I verify Chinese food manufacturer certifications are current and valid?**
All certifications should be verified directly through the issuing certification body's online database or verification service — not via documentation provided by the manufacturer. For Chinese certifications, common verification databases include: CNCA (China National Certification and Accreditation Administration) for CQC certifications, and the specific certification body's own database for SGS, Bureau Veritas, Intertek, and equivalent bodies. WAG conducts this verification as standard practice in all food product sourcing engagements.
