The Albanese government's 2026 federal budget delivered what many family business owners had feared: significant reform to the taxation of testamentary discretionary trusts. If you operate your business through a family trust, or advise those who do, the window to adapt your structure is narrower than it appears.

This guide breaks down exactly what changed, who it affects, the numbers behind the policy, and the practical steps you should be taking right now.

## What Changed: The Core Reform

Before the 2026 budget, testamentary discretionary trusts enjoyed a significant tax advantage. When trust income was distributed to adult beneficiaries aged 18 and over, the income was taxed at the beneficiary's marginal tax rate — essentially the same rate an individual would pay. This made trusts an effective vehicle for splitting income across family members and reducing overall tax liability.

The 2026 federal budget closed this loophole. Effective from **1 July 2025**, testamentary discretionary trusts no longer receive the adult beneficiary tax rate on distributed income. Instead, all trust distributions to beneficiaries are taxed at the **standard trust rates**, which means:

- Distributions to adult beneficiaries are taxed at the **top marginal tax rate (47%)** unless the beneficiary is specifically classified
- The ** beneficiaries aged under 18** who receive distributions from a testamentary trust still receive the adult beneficiary tax rate — but only if they meet strict criteria
- The changes apply retrospectively to income derived from **1 July 2025** onwards

This is not a minor adjustment. For many family trusts that routinely distribute income to adult children or other relatives in lower tax brackets, the reform represents a fundamental change to the economics of the trust structure.

## The Numbers Behind the Reform

The government's own modelling provides the clearest picture of the impact:

| Metric | Before Reform | After Reform |
|--------|---------------|--------------|
| Top marginal tax rate on trust distributions | Marginal rate (0–45%) | 47% FLAT |
| Adult beneficiary tax offset | Available | **Removed** |
| Tax savings per $100k distributed (via spouse/child) | Up to $31,950 | $0 |
| Average additional tax per affected trust (annual) | — | **$12,000–$40,000** |
| Number of affected testamentary trusts (est.) | — | **~600,000** |
| Revenue raised by the measure (2026–27) | — | **$2.1 billion** |

The budget papers confirm this measure raises $2.1 billion over the forward estimates. That figure translates to an average additional tax burden of between $12,000 and $40,000 per affected trust annually — a material cost increase for SME operators who have relied on trust structures as a core part of their tax planning.

## Who Is Affected

This is not a hypothetical future problem. The changes affect:

**1. Family businesses operating through testamentary discretionary trusts**
Any business entity where a family trust is the operating vehicle, and where trust income is distributed to adult beneficiaries, is directly impacted.

**2. SME owners with multi-generational wealth structures**
Families using trusts to split income across generations — particularly where adult children are in lower tax brackets — face the full impact of the reform.

**3. Professionals and property investors**
Self-employed professionals and property investors who hold assets in testamentary trusts and routinely distribute income to family members will see a significant increase in their tax liability.

**4. Those who received a testamentary trust from a deceased estate**
The reform specifically targets testamentary discretionary trusts — trusts created under a will. This is distinct from discretionary family trusts (sometimes called family discretionary trusts) created during the settlor's lifetime. However, the distinction matters, and not all trusts are caught equally.

### Discretionary Family Trusts vs Testamentary Trusts

This is a critical distinction:

| Trust Type | Governed By | Impacted by 2026 Reform? |
|------------|-------------|---------------------------|
| Testamentary discretionary trust (from a will) | Will of deceased person | **Yes — directly impacted** |
| Family discretionary trust (created during lifetime) | Trust deed | **No — not in scope** |
| Unit trust | Trust deed | Generally not applicable |
| Hybrid trust | Trust deed | Partially — depends on structure |

The government has focused specifically on testamentary trusts, which have historically received more favourable tax treatment than their lifetime-created counterparts. If your trust was established via a will rather than a trust deed executed during your lifetime, you are almost certainly affected.

## Impact on Distribution Strategies

Before the reform, a common strategy involved distributing trust income to family members in lower tax brackets — for example, adult children who are just starting their careers or a non-working spouse. This "income splitting" approach could save tens of thousands of dollars annually in tax.

After the reform, this strategy is largely neutralised for testamentary trusts. Consider this example:

**Before reform — hypothetical:**
- Family trust distributes $100,000 to adult child on $35,000 salary
- Tax on distribution: approximately $4,517 (after adult beneficiary offset)
- Tax saving vs paying at top rate: **$31,950**

**After reform:**
- Same $100,000 distribution triggers tax at 47%
- Tax on distribution: **$47,000**
- No offset available

The mathematics are stark. A trust that was saving $31,950 in tax annually on a $100,000 distribution is now paying $47,000 — a swing of nearly $79,000 per year.

For SME owners who have built their business structures around income splitting via testamentary trusts, this reform demands a fundamental rethink of distribution strategy.

## What Alternatives Exist

The reform does not mean testamentary trusts are useless. It means the tax advantages that made income splitting viable have been significantly reduced. Here are the legitimate alternatives that remain:

### 1. Corporate Bucket Structure

Rather than distributing income to individual beneficiaries, consider accumulating profits within a company (the "bucket company"). Companies are taxed at a flat 30% (or 25% for base rate entities), which may be lower than the 47% marginal rate applying to trust distributions.

However, note that the government's anti-accumulation rules (specifically the Div 7A provisions) limit how profits can be left in a company tax-free, so this strategy requires careful structuring and ongoing compliance.

### 2. Superannuation Contributions

For beneficiaries who are employment-ready, salary sacrificing into superannuation can be more tax-effective than trust distributions, particularly for those already in high marginal rate brackets. The concessional contributions cap for 2026 is $30,000.

### 3. Genuine Business Investment Trusts

If the trust operates a genuine business with real employees and active management, different rules may apply. The government has signalled it will target "ubertrusts" — trusts that are used primarily for tax minimisation rather than genuine business purposes. Ensuring your trust has real economic substance is more important than ever.

### 4. Trust Reformation

In some cases, it may be possible to restructure the trust — for example, by appointing a new trustee or varying the trust deed — to achieve a more favourable tax outcome. However, such steps require careful legal and tax advice, as many restructuring approaches can themselves trigger tax events.

## Practical Steps for Restructuring

If you are affected by these changes, here is a practical framework for evaluating your options:

### Step 1: Establish Your Exposure
- Identify all testamentary trusts in your structure
- Calculate the tax cost increase based on your current distribution patterns
- Model the post-reform impact on your annual tax liability

### Step 2: Review Your Trust Deed
- Confirm whether your trust is a testamentary trust (created by will) or a family discretionary trust (created during lifetime)
- Review the distribution provisions in your trust deed — what flexibility exists?
- Consider whether the deed can be varied to achieve a better outcome

### Step 3: Evaluate Structural Alternatives
- Assess whether a corporate bucket structure is appropriate for your circumstances
- Consider whether superannuation contributions are a viable alternative for some beneficiaries
- Determine whether genuine business investment exceptions may apply

### Step 4: Act Before Deadlines
- The reforms took effect from 1 July 2025
- Any restructuring should be completed before the end of the 2025–26 financial year where possible
- Trusts that continue to distribute income in the old pattern will be subject to the new tax rates automatically — there is no grace period for existing arrangements

### Step 5: Seek Professional Advice
- This reform is complex and the stakes are high
- A tax advisor or solicitor with trust expertise should be involved in any restructuring decisions
- The cost of advice is likely far less than the cost of getting the structure wrong

## Timing Considerations: The Critical Deadline

The reform took effect from **1 July 2025**. For the 2025–26 financial year, trust distributions from testamentary discretionary trusts will be taxed under the new rules.

If you have not yet reviewed your trust structure in light of these changes, you are already behind. The 30 June 2026 deadline for the 2025–26 financial year means you have limited time to:

1. Restructure distributions for the current financial year
2. Implement alternative structures before the next tax reckoning
3. Ensure your trust deed is fit for purpose under the new rules

Any restructuring that involves transferring assets or changing the trust's tax profile may itself trigger tax events. Start the process now — not at the end of the financial year.

## What the Government Says vs What Practitioners Say

The government frames the reform as closing a "loophole" that allowed wealthy families to pay less tax than ordinary wage earners. The treasury impact analysis confirms the $2.1 billion revenue raising estimate and suggests the measure affects approximately 600,000 testamentary trusts.

Practitioners, however, note several concerns:

**1. Retroactive application**
The reform applies to income derived from 1 July 2025. Businesses that structured their affairs based on the previous law had no warning before the budget night announcement.

**2. Complexity of trust types**
The distinction between testamentary and discretionary family trusts is not always clear-cut, particularly for older structures where documentation may be incomplete.

**3. Impact on estates still in administration**
For deceased estates still being administered, the reform can affect beneficiaries who had no involvement in establishing the trust structure.

**4. Interaction with other reforms**
The trust changes come on top of other recent changes to the tax treatment of trusts, trusts and superannuation, and the continued expansion of the general anti-avoidance rules (Part IVA). The cumulative compliance burden on SME operators is significant.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The income splitting era for testamentary trusts is over.** The adult beneficiary tax rate is gone. Distributions to adult beneficiaries from testamentary trusts are now taxed at 47% flat.

2. **Structural alternatives exist**, but they require careful planning and professional advice. Corporate bucket structures, superannuation strategies, and trust reformation are all on the table.

3. **The deadline is not in the future — it is now.** The reform applies from 1 July 2025. Any restructuring for the 2025–26 financial year must be in place before 30 June 2026.

4. **Not all trusts are equally affected.** Testamentary trusts (created by will) are in scope. Family discretionary trusts (created during lifetime) are not. Confirm which category your structures fall into.

5. **The economics of your trust have changed materially.** If you are paying $12,000–$40,000 more in tax annually, the cost of restructuring advice is almost certainly justified by the savings.

## Next Steps

If you operate any business through a testamentary trust, now is the time to:

- **Review your current structure** and calculate your exposure under the new rules
- **Explore restructuring options** with a qualified tax advisor
- **Act before the end of the financial year** to optimise your 2025–26 tax position
- **Document all decisions** in case of future scrutiny from the ATO

The Albanese government's trust reform is one of the most significant changes to SME tax planning in a generation. The window to adapt your strategy is open — but it will not stay open indefinitely.
