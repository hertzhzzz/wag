China remains the world's largest garment manufacturer, producing everything from basic t-shirts to high-end designer collections. For Australian fashion brands and clothing retailers sourcing from China, understanding how to verify a garment factory's capabilities is essential.

Our team has visited garment factories across Guangzhou, Dongguan, and the Shanghai region over 8 years. The most common failure mode we see: Australian buyers approve a sample, pay a deposit, and receive bulk production that looks similar but feels different. Fabric weight is lower. Stitching density is reduced. The factory optimised for cost at every step.

This guide covers the key manufacturing hubs, what to verify specific to apparel production, and how to manage the sourcing process.

## Key Manufacturing Hubs

**Guangzhou — Casual and Sportswear.**
Guangzhou's garment district is known for casual wear, sportswear, and fast-fashion production. The Panyu and Baiyun districts host thousands of factories specialising in T-shirts and tops, sportswear and activewear, denim and casual trousers, and light outerwear.

**Dongguan — Higher-Quality Manufacturing.**
Dongguan is where the better-quality factories concentrate — those producing for premium brands and export markets with higher quality specifications. Factories here tend to have better-trained sewing operators, more rigorous QC systems, and experience with stricter compliance requirements.

**Shanghai Region — Premium and Designer.**
Shanghai-area factories cater to higher-end fashion brands and international luxury labels. These factories typically work with finer fabrics and more complex constructions, have lower MOQs (500-1,000 units per style), and charge higher prices but deliver superior quality.

## What to Verify in an Apparel Factory

### Production Capacity and Scale

First, understand whether the factory matches your order size. Ask specifically: what is your monthly production capacity (confirm it exceeds your order volume)? How many operators do you have on the production floor? How many lines can you run simultaneously? What is your current production schedule?

A factory claiming 50,000 units monthly capacity but showing 20 workers on the floor is either misrepresenting itself or subcontracting heavily. Both are red flags.

### Fabric Verification

Fabric quality determines the majority of garment quality. This is where most problems originate.

Ask to verify: fabric composition (what percentage cotton, polyester, etc.?), fabric weight (gsm — grams per square metre), fabric source (domestic Chinese or imported?), and pre-production testing (colour fastness, shrinkage, pilling resistance).

Request fabric specifications before production: mill certificate or test report, composition breakdown, weight and construction details, and care label requirements.

### Construction Quality

Garment construction quality is visible in the details. Stitching should be even and consistent with no loose threads; 12-14 stitches per inch is standard for dress shirts. Seam reinforcement at stress points (crotch, collar, armholes) should have additional reinforcement. Buttons should be securely attached with sufficient stitches to prevent loosening. Finished measurements should be within +/- 1cm of spec.

### QC Systems

Ask specifically: what percentage of garments are inspected at final QC (should be 100% for quality factories)? What is your defect tolerance (industry standard is typically 2-4% AQL)? Do you have in-house QC staff or external inspection (in-house is generally better)? Can I arrange a third-party inspection before shipment (standard practice; any reputable factory will accept)?

## Minimum Order Quantities

MOQs in Chinese garment manufacturing vary by factory type. Basic factories typically require 1,000-3,000 units per style. Mid-range factories: 500-1,000 units. Premium/designer factories: 200-500 units. High-end boutique: 50-200 units.

Factors that affect MOQ include fabric quantity (fabric minimums apply), complexity of construction, number of sizes and colours, and custom labelling or packaging.

Negotiating MOQs is standard practice. Many factories will agree to lower MOQs in exchange for a higher per-unit price or a commitment to larger future orders.

## Australian Compliance Requirements

Australian requirements for imported apparel include: textile labelling (fibre content, care instructions in English), size labelling (must use Australian standard sizes), country of origin (mandatory on label), and fire hazards (for children's sleepwear, specific fire safety standards apply).

Request the factory's experience with Australian labelling requirements before placing your first order. Most factories that export to the US or EU will be familiar with the Australian requirements, but confirm explicitly.

## FAQ

**Can I visit factories before ordering?**
Yes — visiting before your first order is strongly recommended. Most established factories will accommodate a visit. Use the visit to assess their quality standards, production capacity, and communication before committing to production.

**What is the typical lead time for garment production?**
From order confirmation to shipment: 30-45 days for basic styles, 45-60 days for more complex constructions. Add 7-10 days if fabric needs to be sourced specifically for your order. Build in buffer time for sample approval if doing custom development.

**Should I use a sourcing agent for apparel?**
For your first few orders, a sourcing agent with apparel industry experience can be valuable — they understand the right factories for your quality level, can manage production follow-up in Chinese, and can arrange quality inspections. Choose an agent with specific apparel experience; general sourcing agents may not have the technical knowledge to verify construction quality.

**How do I verify fabric quality at source?**
Request the mill certificate and verify it against the mill's records. Ask for fabric composition and weight specifications in writing before production. For critical orders, engage a third-party testing lab to verify fabric specs against the approved sample. Request a production sample before bulk cutting.
