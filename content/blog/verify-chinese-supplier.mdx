## Why Supplier Verification Matters for Australian Importers

When you import from China, the legal responsibility for product quality, compliance, and supply chain integrity rests with you — the Australian importer. Not the factory. Not the freight forwarder. You.

Australian Consumer Law requires that goods meet Australian standards. If you cannot trace your products to a verified manufacturer, you carry all the risk yourself.

The cost of getting this wrong:

- **Deposits lost to shell companies** that take payment and disappear
- **Quality issues** because you never verified the actual production environment
- **Compliance failures** because the factory's certifications were invalid
- **Supply chain disruption** when mid-production you discover the supplier cannot deliver

I have seen all four. None of them are pleasant.

## The 6-Step Verification Framework

### Step 1: Read the Business Scope Carefully

Chinese companies are registered with a specific business scope — a legal description of what the company is authorised to do.

A factory will have manufacturing, production, or processing in its business scope.

<FloatImage src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1565041666698-89c1bb6b2b38?w=800&q=80" alt="Production floor in a manufacturing facility" align="left" width={280} />

A trading company will have wholesale, export, import, or trade — and no manufacturing.

This matters because many suppliers describe themselves as factories without the registration to back it up.

**How to do it:** Search the company on samr.gov.cn (the SAMR registry) using either the company name in Chinese or the unified social credit code. Look for the business scope section. If it says only "wholesale" or "export" and not "manufacturing," you are dealing with a trading company.

**What I have seen:** In three separate cases, suppliers presented themselves as manufacturers but their business scope explicitly described trading operations only. Each time, the clients had already paid deposits.

### Step 2: Verify the Registered Address

The address on a business license must match the actual factory location. If it does not, something is wrong.

<FloatImage src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1581091226825-a6a2a5aee158?w=800&q=80" alt="Manufacturing facility exterior" align="right" width={280} />

**How to do it:** Take the registered address from the business license and put it into Google Maps or Baidu Maps. Look at what is actually at that location. If the address describes an industrial zone but the satellite view shows a commercial building, that is a serious red flag.

**What I have seen:** A supplier's business license showed an address in an industrial park in Shenzhen's Bao'an District. The satellite view showed a commercial office building. When we visited, it was an office — not a factory. The factory photographs on their website belonged to a different company they were affiliated with.

### Step 3: Verify Certificates with the Issuing Body

Certificates can be faked. They can also be expired. Or they can belong to a different company entirely.

Never accept a certificate file as proof of anything. Always verify directly with the issuing organisation.

**How to do it:** Most quality certifications (ISO 9001, CE, CB, etc.) have online verification portals. Every legitimate certification body lets you check certificate numbers. If the supplier cannot provide the certificate number, or if the number does not verify, treat that as a problem.

**What I have seen:** Two cases where the certificate was valid but belonged to a parent company, not the subsidiary actually producing the goods. One case where the certificate was expired by 14 months. One case where the certificate number did not exist in the issuing body's database at all.

### Step 4: Analyse the Product Range and MOQ

Factories tend to be specialised. They invest in equipment and processes for specific product categories.

If a supplier offers lighting, electronics, furniture, and promotional products all from the same factory, they are almost certainly aggregating from multiple manufacturers.

Low MOQ is also a signal. Manufacturing requires setup costs. A genuine factory typically has higher minimums than a trading company that brokers from whatever source is cheapest at the time.

**How to do it:** Ask the supplier directly: "What equipment do you use to produce this?" Ask about specific machine models. Ask about the production line setup. Real factories can answer these questions precisely. Trading companies give vague answers.

**What I have seen:** A supplier advertising 12 different product categories. When I asked about their injection moulding equipment, they could not name a single machine model. They were brokering from multiple factories.

### Step 5: Request Live Factory Proof

Photographs are not proof. Videos can be pre-recorded. Brochures are marketing materials.

The strongest verification is a live video walkthrough of the actual production facility — not a curated tour, but a real-time walkthrough showing active production lines, raw material storage, QC stations, and the packaging area.

<FloatImage src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1523944339745-0f6b27d11ff2?w=800&q=80" alt="Factory inspection and quality audit" align="left" width={280} />

**How to do it:** Request a live video call where the supplier walks through the specific production line that would handle your order. Ask them to show you the machines, the workers, the testing equipment. If they resist or make excuses, that is itself a signal.

**What I have seen:** A supplier who agreed to a video call, then spent 40 minutes showing us their best facilities while claiming the production line for our product category was "under maintenance." The tour looked impressive. The evasion was the information we needed.

### Step 6: Cross-Check Three Addresses

Compare the registered address from the business license, the sample shipment address, and the invoicing company name. If any of these do not match, there is a hidden intermediary in your supply chain.

**How to do it:** When you receive a sample, note where it was shipped from. Compare that city to the registered address on the business license. Compare the invoice or proforma invoice company name to the business license name. If any of these do not align, ask for an explanation before proceeding.

**What I have seen:** A sample shipped from Guangzhou, the business license registered in Shenzhen, and the invoice issued under a third company name. Three different entities, none of them disclosing the connection to the others.

## Red Flags That Should Stop You

These are the signals I have learned to treat as immediate stop signs:

- **The business scope is trading or wholesale, not manufacturing**
- **The registered address shows a commercial building, not an industrial zone**
- **The supplier cannot or will not provide live video of the production line**
- **Certificates do not verify with the issuing body**
- **The sample was shipped from a different city than the factory's registered address**
- **The supplier's company has changed its registered address in the past 12 months**
- **The quoted price is significantly below market rate with no clear cost explanation**
- **The supplier pressures you to decide quickly before you complete verification**

## Before You Send a Deposit

Before you pay anything, you should have confirmed:

<FloatImage src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1565793298595-6a879b1d9492?w=800&q=80" alt="Quality control inspection" align="right" width={280} />

1. **Business license verified** — checked against SAMR registry
2. **Factory address confirmed** — matches an actual manufacturing location
3. **Certifications verified** — checked with issuing bodies, not just provided as files
4. **Live factory evidence** — video walkthrough or on-site visit completed
5. **Company name matches across all documents** — license, invoice, shipping documents

If you cannot confirm all five, do not pay the deposit.

## FAQ

**Can I verify a supplier remotely?**

Yes. Start with the SAMR registry check (Step 1) and address verification (Step 2) — both can be done from Australia in under an hour. For higher-value orders, arrange a live video walkthrough of the production line. For the highest-value orders, plan a physical visit.

**How long does the full verification process take?**

Remote verification takes 1–2 days. A physical factory visit typically takes 5–10 days from initial inquiry to written report. We usually run both in parallel.

**What if the supplier refuses verification?**

Treat refusal as a red flag. Legitimate factories are accustomed to verification requests from international buyers. Reluctance to verify is itself significant information.

**Is a business license enough to prove a supplier is real?**

No. A valid business license only proves the company exists. You still need to verify the business scope, address, and actual production capability.

**What is the biggest mistake Australian buyers make?**

Relying on a professional website, a fluent English speaker, and a good sample without verifying who is actually making the product. This is how deposits get lost.

**How do I verify certificates are genuine and not expired?**

Every legitimate certification body provides online verification portals where you can check certificate numbers. Ask the supplier for their certificate number and verify it directly with the issuing organisation. Do not accept certificate files alone as proof — file copies can be outdated, faked, or belong to a different company.

**What address should I look for on a Chinese business licence?**

The registered address should be in an industrial zone or manufacturing district. If the address shows a commercial office building in a business district, that is a red flag — factories require industrial zoning. Cross-check the registered address on Google Maps or Baidu Maps to confirm what is actually at that location.

**How do I know if a supplier is a trading company posing as a factory?**

Look at the business scope for manufacturing terms. Ask to see the production floor — not the showroom. Ask specific questions about equipment and production processes. A trading company will be vague about manufacturing details while a real factory can describe their processes precisely.

**What should I do before paying any deposit to a Chinese supplier?**

Confirm the business licence against the SAMR registry, verify the factory address matches an industrial location, check all certifications with the issuing bodies, complete a live video walkthrough or physical visit, and confirm the company name matches across all documents including the invoice. If you cannot verify all five, do not pay the deposit.
