## Why Supplier Verification Matters for Australian Importers

For Australian businesses sourcing from China, supplier verification is not just a due diligence exercise. It is a legal and commercial necessity. Once goods are imported into Australia, the responsibility for product quality, compliance, and supply chain integrity rests primarily with the importer.

The cost of getting this wrong is significant:

- **Financial loss**: Deposits paid to shell companies that disappear
- **Quality issues**: Products that do not match specifications because you never verified the actual manufacturer
- **Compliance risks**: Products that fail Australian safety or import regulations
- **Delayed shipments**: Discovering mid-production that the supplier cannot meet your volume or quality requirements

Working with a verified, real factory reduces these risks dramatically. With the right verification process, you can validate suppliers remotely before committing to a visit or a deposit.

## 6 Ways to Check if a Supplier Is a Real Factory

### 1. Read the Business Scope Carefully

A supplier may have a valid business license and still not be a manufacturer. The key is whether its registered business scope includes terms related to production, processing, or manufacturing, rather than only sales, wholesale, or import-export activity.

That distinction matters in practice. Supplier due diligence guidance for buyers sourcing from China specifically recommends using company registration details to determine whether the counterparty is actually a manufacturer or simply a supplier or intermediary.

<Tip>
If a company says it owns a factory but its registration only describes trading, wholesale, and export services, treat that as a warning sign and ask who the real producer is before moving forward.
</Tip>

### 2. Verify the Company in Chinas Official Registry

Chinas National Enterprise Credit Information Publicity System is the official public registry used to check company information. At a minimum, you should verify the legal company name, unified social credit code, registered address, operating status, and any abnormal business notices.

This is an important credibility filter because the system has been a core public verification channel for years. One registry check can confirm at least five essential items at once: legal name, registration code, address, status, and public disclosures. This makes it one of the highest-value first steps in supplier screening.

<Tip>
Search the company on gsxt.gov.cn using the unified social credit code or company name. Look for any abnormal operating status indicators that could signal legal or financial issues.
</Tip>

### 3. Cross-Check the Address Across Three Places

One of the fastest ways to spot a fake factory claim is to compare the registered address, the sample shipment address, and the invoicing or company document address. If those do not line up, there is a real possibility that the sales entity, the factory, and the export entity are all different businesses.

This mismatch matters because many sourcing problems begin with hidden layers between the buyer and the actual producer. Practical import guidance for Australian businesses flags inconsistent documentation and unclear supplier identity as risks that should be investigated before payment.

<Tip>
If the sample is shipped from one city, the business license shows another province, and the proforma invoice comes from a third company name, pause and request full clarification on who manufactures the goods and who will be legally responsible for the order.
</Tip>

### 4. Analyze the Product Range and MOQ

A genuine factory is usually more specialized. If one supplier claims it can manufacture unrelated categories such as lighting, pet products, hardware, and textiles all at once, that often points to a trading company rather than a vertically focused producer.

MOQ can also be a clue. In many sourcing cases, factories tend to have higher minimum order quantities than trading companies. Unusually low MOQ combined with a broad product catalog can suggest that the supplier is aggregating products from multiple factories.

<Tip>
If a supplier offers a very low MOQ, a very broad catalog, and a price well below the market, those three signals together should trigger deeper verification rather than faster payment.
</Tip>

### 5. Request Live Factory Proof

Photos and brochures are easy to curate. A much stronger test is a live video call from the production floor, where the supplier can show machinery, active lines, raw materials, QC stations, and the packaging area in real time.

That is why factory audits remain one of the most important verification tools. Supplier verification guidance emphasizes on-site review because it allows buyers to confirm actual equipment, production capacity, workflow, and quality systems instead of relying on marketing claims.

<Tip>
Request a live video walkthrough of the production facility. Ask to see the machinery, active production lines, raw material storage, and QC stations in real time, not pre-recorded videos.
</Tip>

### 6. Verify Certificates with the Issuing Body

A certificate file by itself does not prove much. Due diligence guidance recommends not only collecting certifications, but also checking them with the issuing organization to confirm that the certificate is valid, current, and issued to the same legal entity you are negotiating with.

This matters because certificate misuse often happens through entity mismatch. If the ISO certificate belongs to a different company name, or the certificate scope does not match the product being offered, that is a strong sign that you are not dealing directly with the true manufacturer.

<Tip>
If the supplier quotes you for electronics, but the certificate belongs to another legal entity or covers unrelated operations, treat that as a material due diligence issue and stop before placing a deposit.
</Tip>

## Red Flags That Suggest a Trading Company or Shell Company

Based on our experience verifying suppliers for Australian buyers, watch for these warning signs:

- **Business scope shows only trading, wholesale, or export, no manufacturing**
- **Company name or address changes between documents**
- **Unusually low MOQ combined with extremely broad product catalog**
- **Price significantly below market rates with no clear cost explanation**
- **Reluctance to provide live video of the production facility**
- **Certificates belong to different company names**
- **Vague or inconsistent answers about production capacity**
- **Pushy sales tactics pressuring you to decide quickly**

## What to Check Before Sending a Deposit

Before paying any deposit to a Chinese supplier, verify these items:

1. **Business license and registry information**: Confirm the company exists and the business scope includes manufacturing
2. **Bank account name matches the company name**: Discrepancies indicate potential fraud
3. **Contract terms clearly specify the manufacturer**: Do not accept we will arrange production
4. **Sample matches your specifications**: Always approve a sample before production
5. **Payment terms protect you**: Consider partial payments tied to milestones
