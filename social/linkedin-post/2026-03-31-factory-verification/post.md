A client paid a $12,000 deposit to a Chinese supplier in March.

By April, the factory had stopped responding.

The supplier had looked professional. English was fluent. The website was polished. The样品 photos were impressive.

Here is what nobody told this client before he sent that deposit:

He was not dealing with a factory. He was dealing with a trading company that brokers orders to whichever manufacturer will accept the lowest price.

When quality problems emerged, the trading company blamed the factory. The factory said they never received the order details. The client absorbed the loss.

This happens to Australian businesses every week. Not because they were careless — because the verification process is not intuitive.

Before paying any deposit overseas, verify three things:

1. Check their business license on gsxt.gov.cn. Look for 生产 (manufacturing) in the scope. If it only shows 贸易 (trade), you have an intermediary.

2. Request a live video of active production lines during working hours. Not a showroom. Not photos. Actual production in motion.

3. Verify every certification directly with the issuing organization. File copies can be fabricated. A phone call to the issuer cannot.

Under Australian Consumer Law, you are the importer of record. That legal responsibility does not transfer to your supplier when things go wrong.

The cost of verification is not zero. The cost of skipping it is everything you paid plus your time, plus the customer relationships you damaged when you could not deliver.

What is the most costly lesson you have learned about supplier verification? I read every comment.
